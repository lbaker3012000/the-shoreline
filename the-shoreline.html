The 7 Best Beaches on the Kent Coast — a Local's Guide

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏖 Beaches📍 Kent coast

Kent has over 350 miles of coastline and more than 50 beaches, with nine holding the Blue Flag award for water quality, safety and facilities in 2025. But the finest stretches aren't always the ones on the tourist boards. We've walked the lot, talked to the locals, and picked the seven that genuinely deliver — dramatic, peaceful, and never far from a great pub.

Practical note — tides matter here

Several of Kent's best beaches are tidal. Botany Bay and Kingsgate Bay can see sand disappear entirely at high tide. Always check before you go — tidetimes.org.uk and the Met Office app are both free and reliable.

1. Botany Bay, near Broadstairs

The chalk stacks at Botany Bay are most dramatic at low tide on a clear morning. Photo: Andras Stefuca / Pexels

If you want one photograph that sums up the Kent coast at its most cinematic, walk down to Botany Bay at low tide on a clear morning. The chalk stacks — those great white columns left behind as the cliff slowly erodes — are among the most striking formations in the south-east, and the sandy bay between them is genuinely beautiful. The rock pools at the base of the cliffs are some of the richest in Thanet, drawing fossil hunters as well as families.

The essentials — Botany Bay

Botany Bay, Cliftonville, Broadstairs

Sandy cove backed by dramatic chalk cliffs — one of Kent's most photographed beaches.

Marine Drive, Cliftonville, Broadstairs CT10 3LG
Getting there: Car park on Botany Road, 10 min walk down cliff path. By train: Broadstairs station (15 min walk).
Best for: Chalk stacks, fossil hunting, rock pools. Dogs: Restricted May–Sep, unrestricted Oct–Apr.

2. Kingsgate Bay, Broadstairs

Kingsgate Bay's chalk sea arch and caves are best explored at low tide.

Tucked between Margate and Broadstairs, Kingsgate Bay is Botany Bay's slightly wilder sibling. The chalk cliffs are just as dramatic, but at low tide the beach reveals a genuine sea arch and a series of caves irresistible to anyone with a torch. The bay stays quieter than Viking Bay because it's harder to find — entirely the point. From here you can walk north to Botany Bay and south toward Joss Bay, the best surf beach in Thanet.

The essentials — Kingsgate Bay

Kingsgate Bay, Broadstairs

Sea caves, a chalk arch, and cliff walks — quieter than Viking Bay year-round.

Kingsgate Avenue, Broadstairs CT10 3QA
Park on Kingsgate Avenue, short footpath to beach. Joss Bay Surf School 5 min walk south. Dogs welcome year-round.

Advertisement — 728×90 Mid-Article (premium placement)

3. Viking Bay, Broadstairs

Viking Bay is Broadstairs at its most quintessentially British — the beach huts and golden sand are a classic combination.

Broadstairs is the classic Kent seaside town — Charles Dickens had a summer home here and you can see why. Viking Bay is the centrepiece: a neat horseshoe of golden sand backed by candy-coloured beach huts, a promenade of independent cafés, and cliffs that glow white in afternoon sun. Blue Flag awarded and lifeguarded in summer.

The essentials — Viking Bay

Viking Bay, Broadstairs

Blue Flag beach, beach huts, lifeguards in summer — Broadstairs' showpiece bay.

Broadstairs town centre CT10 1HX
Pay-and-display parking in town. Direct trains from London St Pancras (1h 20min). For Louisa Bay (quieter alternative) walk south along the cliff.

4. Tankerton Beach, Whitstable

Tankerton's west-facing beach huts catch the full force of a Thames Estuary sunset.

Tankerton is Whitstable's eastern stretch — a long shingle beach lined with the most photogenic beach huts in Kent, facing west over the estuary. On a clear autumn evening this is one of the finest free views on the coast, the light coming gold toward the ghostly Maunsell Sea Forts at Red Sands. At low tide, The Street — a natural shingle ridge — extends half a mile out to sea. Walking it with water on both sides is an entirely memorable experience.

The essentials — Tankerton Beach

Tankerton Beach, Whitstable

Famous beach huts, The Street at low tide, and some of the best sunsets on the coast.

Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE
Parking on Marine Parade. Whitstable station 1.5 miles. Continue east 5 miles to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way.

5. Samphire Hoe, Dover

Samphire Hoe sits at the foot of the White Cliffs of Dover — access is via a tunnel through the chalk.

This one has an extraordinary back story. Samphire Hoe is a nature reserve created from nearly five million cubic metres of chalk excavated to build the Channel Tunnel. Ecologists landscaped it into an undulating coastal shelf and seeded it with wildflowers. Today it hosts over 200 plant species, 220 bird species, and 380 moths. The access road runs through a cliff tunnel, giving arrival a mildly dramatic feeling.

The essentials — Samphire Hoe

Samphire Hoe Nature Reserve, Dover

A remarkable conservation success story beneath the White Cliffs — wheelchair accessible, wildlife-rich, and free.

Western Heights, Dover CT17 9FL
Free car park. Access via cliff tunnel off the A20 — follow signs from Western Docks roundabout. Gate locked at dusk.

Advertisement — 300×250 Inline

6. Stone Bay, Broadstairs

A quiet curve of flat sands just a short walk from Broadstairs town centre. Stone Bay sits sheltered by chalk cliffs creating a slightly warmer microclimate. Blue Flag and Seaside Award both held, it stays noticeably calmer than Viking Bay even at peak summer. Families love it for rock pooling; dog owners for year-round unrestricted access.

The essentials — Stone Bay

Stone Bay, Broadstairs

Stone Road, Broadstairs CT10 1EF
Small car park at the top of the cliff steps. Dogs welcome unrestricted all year. Blue Flag and Seaside Award holder.

7. Sandwich Bay

Access is via a private toll road through a golf course estate — a small fee keeps casual visitor numbers low and the atmosphere wonderfully private. The bay is a long sweep of flat sands and tidal pools backed by dunes. The northern end borders the River Stour and is one of the finest spots on the Kent coast for migrating waders. Pack a thermos — there are no facilities on the beach.

The essentials — Sandwich Bay

Sandwich Bay, Sandwich

Sandwich Bay Road, Sandwich CT13 9PF
Toll road access (small fee per car). Dogs welcome year-round. No facilities on beach — bring everything you need.

The Best Fish & Chip Shops on the Kent Coast — We Ate Them All

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🍟 Food & Drink📍 Kent coast

There is an argument — and we're willing to make it — that a truly great plate of fish and chips on the coast of Kent on a grey October afternoon is one of the finest experiences available in England. Proper batter. Thick chips. Salt air coming through the door. We ate our way from Whitstable in the north to Folkestone in the south.

How we chose these

We visited anonymously, paid our own way, and weighed batter quality, fish freshness, chip thickness, sourcing, and value. We also note the ones to skip.

Whitstable Harbour — start of any serious Kent seafood tour.

Whitstable

⭐ Our top pick in Whitstable — since 1962

VC Jones — Traditional Fish & Chips

A genuine institution. The Times has rated it one of the best fish and chip shops in the UK. VC Jones is one of the last chippies in the south of England still frying in beef dripping, maintaining a tradition that predates the Second World War. A three-generation family business. The fat gives the batter a depth and richness that vegetable oil cannot replicate. Food is donated to local projects and cooking fat is recycled. Queue expected at weekends. Cash preferred.

25 Harbour Street, Whitstable CT5 1AH
Tel: 01227 272703 · vcjones.co.uk
Mon–Sat 11:30am–8pm · Sun 12–7pm (hours seasonal, check website)

Also excellent in Whitstable

Ossie's Best Fish and Chips

Sunday Times mentioned and multiple Tripadvisor Travellers' Choice awards. The batter is thin and genuinely crisp — the kind that shatters rather than bends. Generous portions. A short walk from Tankerton Beach makes this the ideal eat-then-walk evening stop. The 2024 Tripadvisor Travellers' Choice Award winner.

205 Tankerton Road, Whitstable CT5 2AT
Tel: 01227 266600 · ossiesfishandchips.com
Tue–Sun 12–8pm (table bookings recommended)

Upmarket option — Whitstable's oldest restaurant

Wheelers Oyster Bar

Not a chippy in the traditional sense, but the fish is superb — served from behind a distinctive pink-and-blue Victorian shopfront that's been here since 1856. The kitchen now turns out lobster lasagne and scallops alongside battered fish. Just four tables inside. BYOB (no corkage). The Foodie Travel Guide calls it "a near mystical seafood dining experience."

8 High Street, Whitstable CT5 1BQ
Tel: 01227 273311 · wheelersoysterbar.com
Mon–Tue 10:30am–9pm · Thu–Fri 10:15am–9:30pm · Sat 10am–10pm · Wed & Sun: Closed. Bookings essential.

Left: a proper battered cod — the benchmark. Right: Whitstable harbour on a summer evening.

Broadstairs

⭐ Michelin Guide listed — Broadstairs

Kebbells Seafood Bar & Restaurant

Overlooking Viking Bay, Kebbells isn't doing traditional fish and chips — it's doing something better. Small and large sharing plates with a Mediterranean influence, seasonal menus built around locally sourced fish. The stone bass is the dish people come back for. Listed in the Michelin Guide and winner of the Kent Food & Drink Awards Restaurant of the Year. The terrace in summer, looking down over the bay, is one of the best dining spots in Thanet. Book well ahead at weekends.

8 Victoria Parade, Broadstairs CT10 1QS
Tel: 01843 319002 · kebbells.com
Tue–Fri 12–3pm & 6–9pm · Sat–Sun 12–9pm · Mon: Closed. Reservations recommended.

Margate

Margate beach — Peter's Fish Factory is on The Parade, steps from the sand since 1984.

⭐ Our top pick in Margate — since 1984

Peter's Fish Factory

A Margate institution since 1984. On the beach, always busy, and the fish and chips are exactly what they should be: fresh, hot, unpretentious, and good value. The queue is both a warning and a recommendation. Come early or late — mid-afternoon on a summer Saturday is not the moment.

12 The Parade, Margate CT9 1EZ
Open daily 11am–11pm · Takeaway and eat-in available · Outdoor seating facing the beach

Michelin Green Star · For something different — Margate

Angela's (and Dory's)

Angela's is a Michelin Green Star-awarded small seafood restaurant — the award recognising exceptional sustainable practices. No meat on the menu. All seafood sourced from local dayboats, supply chain largely plastic-free. The daily changing menu follows what the boats bring in each morning. Its sister Dory's is a seafood bar two doors along, serving raw, pickled and baked plates, walk-ins only. Both are outstanding.

Angela's: 21 The Parade, Margate CT9 1EX
Tel: 01843 319978 · angelasofmargate.com
Tue–Sat: Lunch from 12pm, Dinner from 6pm. Bookings recommended.
Dory's (wine bar/seafood): Thu–Mon 12–9pm · Walk-ins only

Advertisement — 300×250 Inline

Folkestone

Folkestone Harbour — The Smokehouse is on Back Street, beside the historic Stade fishing quarter.

⭐ Our top pick in Folkestone — one of The Times' top 30

The Smokehouse

Opened by chef Mark Sargeant (a former Gordon Ramsay associate) in a 150-year-old building beside Folkestone harbour. Battered cod or haddock alongside beef dripping chips, salt and pepper squid to start, and a proper wine list. The Times named it one of the 30 best fish and chip shops in Britain. The five-metre glass frontage lets in the cobbled Stade and harbour views. Fish from Kent day boats.

1–3 Back Street, Folkestone CT19 6NN
Tel: 01303 884718 · thesmokehousefolkestone.co.uk
Tue–Sun 12–4pm & 5–9pm · Mon: Closed

A note on prices in 2026

A standard cod and chips runs £12–£16 across these places. For best value, Peter's Fish Factory in Margate consistently delivers the most for your money. Expect to pay more at Kebbells and Angela's — but these are genuinely different experiences, not just fish and chips.

Whitstable to Herne Bay: the Perfect Kent Coastal Walk

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🚶 Walks📍 North Kent

Five miles. Entirely flat. Trains at both ends. A row of the most photographed beach huts in England, two fine pubs, excellent views across the Thames Estuary, and — on a clear day — the rusting outline of the Red Sands Maunsell Sea Forts standing sentinel in the water. The walk from Whitstable to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way is one of the most satisfying and accessible day walks in the south-east.

The quick facts

Distance: 5 miles (8 km) · Terrain: Flat shingle coastal path, some pavement · Time: 2–2.5 hours walking · Start: Whitstable Harbour (train: Whitstable station, 15 min walk) · End: Herne Bay Pier (train: Herne Bay station, 10 min walk) · Dogs: Yes, welcome year-round on most of the route

Start at Whitstable Harbour — arrive early for oysters or coffee at the harbour cafés before setting off east.

Getting there

The walk works best as a one-way trip by train. Southeastern services run from London Victoria or St Pancras into Whitstable (approx 1h 20min from St Pancras). Walk east to Herne Bay and catch the return train. By car, park in Herne Bay (easier and cheaper at weekends) and take the train one stop to Whitstable to start.

The route

Stage 1 — 1.5 miles

Whitstable Harbour to Tankerton

The path follows shingle east from the harbour. On your right is The Street — a natural shingle ridge extending half a mile out to sea at low tide, worth walking if conditions allow. Shortly after, the famous Tankerton beach huts begin: painted in heritage colours and facing west over the estuary, best in late afternoon golden light. Tankerton Slopes — the green lawns above the beach — are perfect for picnics.

Left: Tankerton's beach huts in the late afternoon. Right: the shingle shore looking east toward Herne Bay.

Best food stop on the route

JoJo's Restaurant

The best food stop on the walk. JoJo's sits at the top of Tankerton Slopes looking over the estuary and serves a short but excellent menu of seasonal dishes — relaxed, confident cooking that Whitstable does better than anywhere. Lunch is the right meal here.

2 Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE
Book ahead at weekends — it fills up fast with walkers who know the route.

Stage 2 — 1.5 miles

Swalecliffe and Hampton Pier

After Tankerton the path continues through Swalecliffe, crossing Swalecliffe Brook and passing Long Rock — a small nature reserve and Site of Special Scientific Interest home to sea birds and the rare Fisher's estuarine moth. Hampton Pier is a quiet fishing spot. The views east from here, with Herne Bay's Victorian skyline growing on the horizon, are among the best on the walk.

Looking east from Hampton Pier — the Thames Estuary opens out and Herne Bay's clock tower appears ahead.

Stage 3 — 2 miles

Herne Bay Pier and finish

The approach to Herne Bay is along the seafront promenade. The town's 80-foot Victorian Clock Tower — built in 1837, one of the earliest freestanding purpose-built clock towers in the UK — announces your arrival. Herne Bay Pier stretches into the estuary: the detached head visible some distance from shore is what remains after a storm destroyed the centre section. Walk to the end for a coffee and the best views back along the coast you've just walked.

Where to eat and drink in Herne Bay

Best pub at the finish

The Four Fathoms

Voted Kent Life Pub of the Year. A large courtyard garden, good food, and a comfortable welcome that's exactly what a tired and windswept walker needs. Also: Beer on the Pier for a drink at the end of the pier itself before catching the train.

The Four Fathoms: High Street, Herne Bay CT6 5LH
Beer on the Pier: Central Parade, Herne Bay CT6 5JJ

Advertisement — 728×90 Mid-Article

Extending the walk

This stretch is part of the Saxon Shore Way, a 163-mile long-distance route from Gravesend to Hastings tracing the coastline as it existed in Roman times. Extending east from Herne Bay to Reculver — about 4 miles — is arguably more dramatic, with the twin towers of the Roman fort appearing on the horizon. From Reculver, the Thanet Coastal Path links onward toward Birchington, Margate and Broadstairs.

Good to know

Comfortable walking shoes are fine — boots unnecessary except in wet weather. Almost entirely flat. Dogs welcome on all but a short stretch near a holiday park at Swalecliffe.

The Best Dog-Friendly Beaches on the Kent Coast

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🐾 Dogs📍 Kent coast

Kent is one of the best counties in England for dogs on the coast. The shingle beaches are easy on paws, the towns are broadly welcoming to four-legged visitors, and outside the summer season you can often walk for miles without meeting another soul. But seasonal restrictions apply on many of the most popular beaches — knowing the rules before you go saves disappointment.

How dog rules work on Kent beaches

Most Kent beaches operate restrictions between 1 May and 30 September (roughly). During this period, dogs are either banned entirely or confined to a small section. Between October and April, restrictions are lifted and most beaches are fully open. Rules vary beach by beach, and some council boundaries differ slightly — always check the local council website for the current year's rules.

Autumn and winter are the finest seasons for dogs on the Kent coast — unrestricted beaches, empty sands, and low golden light.

Year-round dog beaches (no seasonal restrictions)

These are the genuinely unrestricted beaches — dogs welcome every day of the year, no seasonal bans. They are also, not coincidentally, excellent beaches in their own right.

1. Stone Bay, Broadstairs

The best year-round dog beach in Thanet, and probably in Kent. Stone Bay sits in a sheltered chalk cove just south of Broadstairs town centre — easy to reach on foot, never as busy as Viking Bay, and holding both Blue Flag and Seaside Award status. The chalk cliffs above create a windbreak effect that makes it feel warmer than surrounding beaches. The rock pools are rich and endlessly interesting to dogs with any instinct for investigation. Unlike many Thanet beaches, there are no seasonal restrictions at all — Stone Bay is open to dogs every single day of the year.

The essentials — Stone Bay

Stone Bay, Broadstairs

Sheltered chalk cove, Blue Flag, year-round dog access — the best all-seasons dog beach in Thanet.

Stone Road, Broadstairs CT10 1EF
Small car park at the clifftop. Steps down to beach — not suitable for pushchairs. Dogs: unrestricted year-round. No ban, ever.

2. Sandwich Bay

One of Kent's most unusual beaches — access is via a private toll road through a golf estate (small fee per car), which naturally keeps visitor numbers low. The result is a vast, flat sweep of dunes, tidal pools, and sand that feels genuinely wild, backed by the river Stour to the north and the English Channel to the east. Dogs are welcome year-round with no restrictions whatsoever. There are no facilities (no café, no toilets, no bins — pack everything out), but the space and the birdlife more than compensate. For a dog that loves to run, Sandwich Bay is about as good as it gets on this stretch of coast.

The essentials — Sandwich Bay

Sandwich Bay, Sandwich

Wild, private, unlimited space — dogs unrestricted year-round. No facilities, but pack a flask and it's perfect.

Sandwich Bay Road, Sandwich CT13 9PF
Toll road access (small fee). Dogs welcome year-round, no ban, no lead requirements. Bring water, bags, and everything else you need.

3. Kingsdown Beach, near Deal

A long, quiet shingle beach running south from the village of Kingsdown toward Walmer. It's one of the least visited stretches of the Kent coast — no facilities, no car park right on the beach, and no summer crowds. Dogs are welcome year-round with no restrictions. The shingle gives way to sand at low tide, and the views across the Channel to France are among the clearest on the coast. The village pub, the Zetland Arms, sits right above the beach and welcomes dogs inside as well as in the garden.

The essentials — Kingsdown Beach

Kingsdown Beach, near Deal

Quiet, shingle, year-round dog access — and one of the best dog-friendly pubs in Kent directly above it.

Beach Road, Kingsdown, Deal CT14 8AF
Limited roadside parking in Kingsdown village. Dogs year-round, no restrictions. The Zetland Arms (pub directly above beach) is dog-friendly inside and out.

4. Samphire Hoe, Dover

Technically not a traditional beach — Samphire Hoe is a nature reserve built from Channel Tunnel spoil, with a flat coastal path rather than a conventional strand. But it is one of the best places on the Kent coast to walk a dog, year-round and without restriction. The reserve is fully flat, wheelchair and dog-accessible, and fenced from the cliff edge. Dogs can be let off the lead in most areas (except the reserve sections closest to the railway line). The scenery — white cliffs towering above, ferries tracking across the Channel below — is genuinely extraordinary.

The essentials — Samphire Hoe

Samphire Hoe Nature Reserve, Dover

Flat, fenced, extraordinary cliff scenery — one of the finest year-round dog walks on the whole Kent coast.

Western Heights, Dover CT17 9FL
Free car park. Access via cliff tunnel off the A20. Dogs welcome year-round, off-lead in most areas. Gate locked at dusk.

Beaches with seasonal restrictions (but excellent in winter)

These beaches ban or restrict dogs between roughly May and September, but are among the very best outside that window — and in winter they are outstanding.

5. Botany Bay, Broadstairs (Oct–Apr only)

Botany Bay is one of Kent's most spectacular beaches — chalk stacks, rock pools, sand. It's also one of the richest sniffing environments on the coast, with seaweed, chalk, rockpool life, and the occasional stranded cuttlebone. The restriction runs from 1 May to 30 September. Between October and April it's fully open to dogs, and a low-tide winter morning here — chalk stacks catching the frost, beach empty — is one of the finest dog walk experiences in the south-east.

Dogs: Oct–Apr unrestricted · May–Sep banned

Botany Bay, Broadstairs CT10 3LG

Spectacular chalk stacks and rich rock pools — one of Kent's finest winter dog beaches.

Marine Drive, Cliftonville, Broadstairs CT10 3LG
Car park on Botany Road, 10 min cliff path to beach. Unrestricted Oct–Apr. Total ban May–Sep.

6. Tankerton Beach, Whitstable (Oct–Apr off-lead)

Tankerton's shingle strip is a great dog walk at any time of year — the beach is long enough that you can always find a quiet stretch, the famous beach huts give the walk a distinctly British character, and The Street (the shingle causeway that extends half a mile out to sea at low tide) is an unforgettable experience for a dog who's not alarmed by water on both sides. Dogs are banned from the main stretch in summer but welcome off-lead between October and April. In winter, the west-facing beach catches the low afternoon light magnificently.

Dogs: Oct–Apr unrestricted · May–Sep banned (main stretch)

Tankerton Beach, Whitstable CT5 2BE

Beach huts, The Street at low tide, and glorious sunset walks — ideal for dogs in the off-season.

Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE
Parking on Marine Parade. Dogs fully off-lead Oct–Apr. Summer ban on main stretch — a small section at the far western end stays open year-round.

Dog-friendly cafés along the coast

Most independent cafés on the Kent coast welcome dogs, especially outside summer. Reliable options: The Neptune pub, Whitstable Harbour (dogs welcome inside and in garden); Whitstable Castle café (garden, dogs welcome); The Funky Mackerel, Deal Pier (outdoor terrace, dogs welcome); Kiosk at Walmer Beach (open year-round, outdoor seating). Always call ahead in summer as policies can change.

What to pack for a day's beach walk with a dog

Kent's shingle beaches are generally safe for dogs but there are a few things worth knowing: shingle can get hot in direct sun in summer (check with the back of your hand before letting a dog lie down); some beaches have groynes at low tide that can cut paws; and the tidal range is significant — always check before taking a dog near the base of chalk cliffs, where rock falls are more common than they look. A long lead is useful for beaches with livestock access restrictions nearby, and a collapsible water bowl is worth carrying from May onwards when fresh water is harder to find.

Deal: the Best Town on the Kent Coast You Haven't Visited Yet

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏘️ Towns📍 East Kent

Deal is quietly becoming one of the most talked-about small towns on the south coast. It has everything the more famous Kent seaside towns have — Georgian architecture, a decent pier, pebble beaches, good pubs — but without the day-tripper crowds that make Whitstable difficult on a summer Saturday. It's the kind of place where you can still get a table at lunch without a booking, where the coffee shop knows your name by your second visit, and where the house prices tell you it's been discovered by people who know what they're looking for.

Getting to Deal

Direct trains from London St Pancras take about 1h 40min via Folkestone or Ramsgate. By car, Deal is just off the A258 between Dover and Sandwich — about 15 minutes from the M20. Parking is easy and mostly free on the residential streets west of the seafront.

Deal's fishing boats are still working boats — the beach launch tradition dates back centuries.

What makes Deal different

Most Kent seaside towns pivoted hard to tourism decades ago. Deal didn't. It has a working fishing beach — boats are still launched from the shingle in the old way, and fresh fish is sold directly from the boats on the beach. The town centre is a genuine high street with independent butchers, bakers, a proper hardware shop, and a record store (Pilot). The pubs are locals' pubs that happen to welcome visitors, not visitor pubs with locals' prices.

The seafront is also unusual. There's no promenade funfair, no amusement arcades, no candyfloss stalls. What there is: a long, flat shingle beach good for walking; a Victorian pier that's one of only a handful of intact fishing piers left in England; the ruins of a Napoleonic-era military barracks at Walmer; and the extraordinary time-capsule of Deal Castle, built by Henry VIII in the shape of a Tudor rose and still one of the most intact artillery castles in England.

Where to eat

⭐ Our top pick — Deal's best restaurant

Frog & Scot

A Franco-Scottish bistro that sounds like a joke and delivers something genuinely excellent. A short, seasonal menu built around Kent and Scottish produce — typically five or six starters, five or six mains. The cooking is confident and unfussy: moules marinière done properly, Shetland salmon, a rotating selection of local cheeses. BYO wine (minimal corkage) keeps the bill reasonable. It fills up fast and doesn't take bookings online — call or turn up early.

86 High Street, Deal CT14 6EG
Tel: 01304 379444
Tue–Sat: Lunch 12–2pm, Dinner 6–9pm · Sun lunch only · Mon: Closed. Bookings recommended.

Best seafront café

The Strand

Directly on the seafront, housed in a listed Georgian building with views straight out to the Goodwin Sands. The Strand does the things a good beach café should do — proper coffee, freshly made sandwiches, excellent fish finger baps — but does them better than you'd expect. The sun terrace in summer is the best outdoor seat in Deal. In winter, the interior with its original Georgian windows looking out to sea is almost better.

The Strand, Deal CT14 7AA
Open daily from 9am. Dogs welcome on the terrace. Walk-ins only.

Where to drink

Best pub — Deal

The Ship Inn

The best pub in Deal, possibly the best pub in East Kent. A genuine free house on the seafront — no chain ownership, no fruit machines, no background music loud enough to prevent conversation. The beer range changes constantly and leans hard toward local Kent breweries: Goacher's, Ramsgate Brewery, Old Dairy. The food is a short, honest menu that changes daily. In summer the upstairs terrace looks directly out over the English Channel toward France. In winter, the coal fire is lit and the regulars are exactly the kind of company you want in a pub on a January evening.

141 Middle Street, Deal CT14 6JZ
Open daily from 12pm. Dogs welcome. No bookings — first come, first served.

Honourable mention

The Zetland Arms, Kingsdown

Technically in the village of Kingsdown rather than Deal itself, but only a 25-minute clifftop walk south along the coastal path. The Zetland Arms sits directly above Kingsdown Beach with a terrace that looks straight across the water. It's a proper pub — good local ales, simple food, no pretension — and the walk there along the white-cliff path from Deal Pier is one of the finest 45-minute walks in Kent. Dogs welcome inside and out.

Wellington Parade, Kingsdown, Deal CT14 8AF
Open daily from 12pm. Dogs welcome. Terrace has direct sea views.

What to see and do

Historic site — Deal

Deal Castle

One of the finest Tudor military buildings in England, and criminally under-visited. Henry VIII built Deal Castle in 1540 as part of a chain of artillery fortresses to defend against a French invasion — the shape, seen from above, is a perfect Tudor rose. The stonework is remarkably intact, the chambers and tunnels are explorable, and the moat is still there. English Heritage manages it and entry is under £10. On a weekday outside summer you can often have the whole place almost to yourself, which makes it feel genuinely eerie in the best possible way.

Victoria Road, Deal CT14 7BA
Open Apr–Oct daily 10am–6pm · Nov–Mar Sat & Sun only 10am–4pm
Adult entry approx £9. English Heritage members free. Dogs allowed in grounds but not inside.

Historic site — Walmer, 1 mile south

Walmer Castle & Gardens

A mile south of Deal along the seafront path, Walmer Castle is Deal's quieter and in some ways more interesting counterpart. Also built by Henry VIII, it was later converted into the official residence of the Lord Warden of the Cinque Ports — a position once held by the Duke of Wellington, whose rooms and effects remain on display. The formal gardens, redesigned in the 19th century, are genuinely beautiful in spring and summer. The combined Deal–Walmer walk along the seafront and back through the town is about 4 miles and takes in the best of both places.

Kingsdown Road, Walmer, Deal CT14 7LJ
Open Apr–Oct daily 10am–6pm. Combined ticket with Deal Castle available. Gardens free to visit separately.

Deal Pier: a proper fishing pier

Deal Pier is one of only a handful of traditional fishing piers left in England that is still used primarily for fishing rather than entertainment. There are no amusements, no arcades, no dodgems — just a 300-metre concrete walkway extending into the Channel with a kiosk at the end selling tea and bait. In summer, the pier is lined with anglers catching bass, sole, and mackerel from the beach end and cod further out. The views back to the town from the pier head — the Georgian seafront, the castle, the shingle beach — are the best views of Deal there are.

The best time to visit Deal

September and October are exceptional — the summer visitors have gone, the weather is still reasonable, and the town relaxes back into itself. The seafood is at its best (bass season is September–November), the pubs are quieter, and the light over the Channel in early autumn is the best photographic light on the coast. If you come in summer, arrive on a weekday and before 11am to park easily.

Deal's back streets are full of well-preserved Georgian terraces — the town was prosperous enough to build well, quiet enough that it was never knocked down and rebuilt.

A day itinerary for Deal

Morning: Arrive by train, walk the seafront south to Walmer Castle (1 mile). Explore the castle and gardens, then walk back along Deal beach. Stop at The Strand for coffee and a fish finger bap looking out to the Goodwin Sands.

Afternoon: Deal Castle (30–45 min). Browse the high street — Pilot Records is worth twenty minutes of any music lover's time. Walk the pier to the end and back.

Evening: The Ship Inn for local ales, then Frog & Scot for dinner (book ahead). Last train to London from Deal around 9.30pm — check timetable in advance.
