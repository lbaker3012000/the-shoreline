The 7 Best Beaches on the Kent Coast — a Local's Guide

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏖 Beaches📍 Kent coast

Kent has over 350 miles of coastline and more than 50 beaches, with nine holding the Blue Flag award for water quality, safety and facilities in 2025. But the finest stretches aren't always the ones on the tourist boards. We've walked the lot, talked to the locals, and picked the seven that genuinely deliver — dramatic, peaceful, and never far from a great pub.

Practical note — tides matter here

Several of Kent's best beaches are tidal. Botany Bay and Kingsgate Bay can see sand disappear entirely at high tide. Always check before you go — tidetimes.org.uk and the Met Office app are both free and reliable.

1. Botany Bay, near Broadstairs

The chalk stacks at Botany Bay are most dramatic at low tide on a clear morning.

If you want one photograph that sums up the Kent coast at its most cinematic, walk down to Botany Bay at low tide on a clear morning. The chalk stacks — those great white columns left behind as the cliff slowly erodes — are among the most striking formations in the south-east, and the sandy bay between them is genuinely beautiful. The rock pools at the base of the cliffs are some of the richest in Thanet, drawing fossil hunters as well as families. There's a café above the bay, toilets, and a bus stop five minutes away. The one caveat: the tide comes in fast — don't get absorbed in a rock pool and lose track of it.

The essentials — Botany Bay

Marine Drive, Cliftonville, Broadstairs CT10 3LG

Getting there: No direct parking at the beach — use Botany Road car park and walk 10 min down the cliff path. By train: Broadstairs station, 15 min walk or short cab. Best for: Chalk stacks, fossil hunting, rock pools. Dogs: Restricted May–Sep, unrestricted Oct–Apr.

2. Kingsgate Bay, Broadstairs

Tucked between Margate and Broadstairs, Kingsgate Bay is Botany Bay's slightly wilder sibling. The chalk cliffs are just as dramatic, but at low tide the beach reveals a genuine sea arch and a series of caves irresistible to anyone with a torch. The bay stays quieter than Viking Bay because it's harder to find — entirely the point. From here you can walk north to Botany Bay and south toward Joss Bay, the best surf beach in Thanet.

The essentials — Kingsgate Bay

Kingsgate Avenue, Broadstairs CT10 3QA

Getting there: Park on Kingsgate Avenue. Short footpath walk to beach. Best for: Sea caves, cliff walks, photography, year-round dog walking. Also nearby: Joss Bay Surf School, 5 min south.

3. Viking Bay, Broadstairs

Viking Bay is Broadstairs at its most quintessentially British — arrive early in summer to secure a spot.

Broadstairs is the classic Kent seaside town — Charles Dickens had a summer home here and you can see why. Viking Bay is the centrepiece: a neat horseshoe of golden sand backed by candy-coloured beach huts, a promenade of independent cafés, and cliffs that glow white in afternoon sun. Blue Flag awarded and lifeguarded in summer. For a quieter alternative, walk south to Louisa Bay — a hidden pocket of sand that sees a fraction of Viking Bay's crowds.

The essentials — Viking Bay

Broadstairs town centre CT10 1HX

Getting there: Pay-and-display in town. Direct trains from London St Pancras (1h 20 min). Best for: Classic seaside, families, Blue Flag swimming. Eat nearby: Kebbells seafood — sharing plates, locally sourced fish, book ahead.

4. Tankerton Beach, Whitstable

Tankerton's west-facing beach huts catch the full force of a Thames Estuary sunset.

Tankerton is Whitstable's eastern stretch — a long shingle beach lined with arguably the most photogenic beach huts in Kent, facing west over the estuary. On a clear autumn evening this is one of the finest free views on the coast, the light coming gold toward the ghostly Maunsell Sea Forts at Red Sands. At low tide, The Street — a natural shingle ridge — extends half a mile out to sea. Walking it with water on both sides is an entirely memorable experience.

The essentials — Tankerton Beach

Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE

Getting there: Parking on Marine Parade (some free, some pay). Whitstable station 1.5 miles. Best for: Sunsets, beach hut photography, The Street at low tide. Walk extension: Continue east 5 miles to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way.

5. Samphire Hoe, Dover

Samphire Hoe sits at the foot of the White Cliffs of Dover — access is via a tunnel through the chalk.

This one has an extraordinary back story. Samphire Hoe is a nature reserve created from nearly five million cubic metres of chalk excavated to build the Channel Tunnel. Rather than dumping it at sea, engineers and ecologists landscaped it into an undulating coastal shelf and seeded it with wildflowers. Today it hosts over 200 plant species, 220 bird species, and 380 moths. The access road runs through a cliff tunnel, which gives arrival a mildly dramatic feeling.

The essentials — Samphire Hoe

Western Heights, Dover CT17 9FL

Getting there: Free car park. Access via tunnel through cliffs off the A20 — follow signs from Western Docks roundabout. Best for: Wildlife, photography, wheelchair access. Note: Gate locked at dusk — check seasonal closing times at entrance.

6. Stone Bay, Broadstairs

A quiet curve of flat sands just a short walk from Broadstairs town centre. Stone Bay sits sheltered by chalk cliffs creating a slightly warmer microclimate. Blue Flag and Seaside Award both awarded, it stays noticeably calmer than Viking Bay even at peak summer. Families love it for rock pooling; dog owners for year-round access. It never quite fills up.

The essentials — Stone Bay

Stone Road, Broadstairs CT10 1EF

Getting there: Small car park at the top of the cliff steps. Best for: Quiet rock pooling, families. Dogs: Yes, unrestricted all year.

7. Sandwich Bay

Access is via a private toll road through a golf course estate — a small fee keeps casual visitor numbers low and the atmosphere wonderfully private. The bay is a long sweep of flat sands and tidal pools backed by dunes, with extraordinary birdlife: the northern end borders the River Stour and is one of the finest spots on the Kent coast for migrating waders. Pack a thermos — there are no facilities on the beach.

The essentials — Sandwich Bay

Sandwich Bay Road, Sandwich CT13 9PF

Getting there: Toll road access (small fee per car). Best for: Birding, solitude, long walks. Dogs: Welcome year-round. Facilities: None — bring everything.

The Best Fish & Chip Shops on the Kent Coast — We Ate Them All

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🍟 Food & Drink📍 Kent coast

There is an argument — and we're willing to make it — that a truly great plate of fish and chips on the coast of Kent on a grey October afternoon is one of the finest experiences available in England. Proper batter. Thick chips. Salt air coming through the door. We set out to find the best, eating our way from Whitstable in the north to Folkestone in the south.

How we chose these

We visited anonymously, paid our own way, and weighed batter quality, fish freshness, chip thickness, sourcing, and value. We also note the ones to skip — life is too short for a disappointing chippy by the sea.

Whitstable Harbour at dusk — the starting point of any serious Kent seafood tour.

Whitstable

⭐ Our top pick in Whitstable

VC Jones — 8 Harbour Street, Whitstable CT5 1AJ

A genuine institution. The Times has rated it one of the best fish and chip shops in the UK. VC Jones is one of the last chippies in the south of England still frying in beef dripping, maintaining a tradition that predates the Second World War. A three-generation family business serving since 1962. The fat gives the batter a depth and richness that vegetable oil cannot replicate. Queue expected at weekends. Cash preferred.

Also excellent

Ossie's Best Fish and Chips — 205 Tankerton Road, Whitstable CT5 2AT

Out on the Tankerton Road, Ossie's has earned a Sunday Times mention and multiple Tripadvisor Travellers' Choice awards. The batter is thin and genuinely crisp — the kind that shatters rather than bends. A short walk from Tankerton Beach makes this the obvious choice for an eat-then-walk evening.

Upmarket option

Wheelers Oyster Bar — 8 High Street, Whitstable CT5 1BQ

Not a chippy in the traditional sense, but the fish is superb — served from behind a pink Victorian shopfront that's been here since 1856. The kitchen now turns out lobster lasagne alongside battered fish. Small, intimate, bookings essential.

Left: a proper battered cod — the benchmark. Right: Margate's seafront on a summer afternoon.

Broadstairs

⭐ Our top pick in Broadstairs

Kebbells Seafood Bar — 14–16 Albion Street, Broadstairs CT10 1LU

Overlooking Viking Bay, Kebbells isn't doing traditional fish and chips — it's doing something better. Small and large sharing plates with a Mediterranean influence, seasonal menus built around locally sourced fish. The stone bass is the dish people come back for. The terrace in summer, looking down over the bay, is one of the best dining spots in Thanet. Book well ahead at weekends.

Margate

Margate beach — Peter's Fish Factory is on Marine Terrace, steps from the sand.

⭐ Our top pick in Margate

Peter's Fish Factory — Marine Terrace, Margate CT9 1XJ

Peter's has been feeding Margate since 1984 and is as close to a town institution as a chippy can get. On the beach, always busy, fresh, hot, unpretentious, and good value. The queue snaking from the door is both a warning and a recommendation. Come early or late — mid-afternoon on a summer Saturday is not the moment.

For something different

Angela's / Dory's — 21a The Parade, Margate CT9 1EX

Angela's is a much-lauded small seafood restaurant with rooms. Its sister Dory's overlooks Margate Main Sands and doubles as a wine shop specialising in organic and biodynamic bottles. Direct sourcing from local fishermen, zero plastic in the supply chain. Not a chippy — but the best fish experience in Margate.

Advertisement — 300×250 Inline

Folkestone

⭐ Our top pick in Folkestone

The Smokehouse — The Harbour, Folkestone CT20 1QH

The Smokehouse puts a distinctive twist on the chippy formula: battered cod or haddock alongside beef dripping chips, starters of salt and pepper squid, and a proper wine list. The Times named it one of the 30 best fish and chip shops in Britain. The harbour views do the decorating.

The ones to skip

As a general rule: anything with a laminated picture menu in the window and pre-cooked fish sitting under a heat lamp is one to walk past. The quality gap between best and worst on the Kent coast is substantial.

A note on prices in 2026

A standard cod and chips runs £12–£16 across these places. For best value on the coast, Peter's Fish Factory in Margate consistently delivers the most for your money.

Whitstable to Herne Bay: the Perfect Kent Coastal Walk

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🚶 Walks📍 North Kent

Five miles. Entirely flat. Trains at both ends. A row of the most photographed beach huts in England, two fine pubs, excellent views across the Thames Estuary, and — on a clear day — the rusting outline of the Red Sands Maunsell Sea Forts standing sentinel in the water. The walk from Whitstable to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way is, by some margin, one of the most satisfying and accessible day walks in the south-east.

The quick facts

Distance: 5 miles (8 km) · Terrain: Flat shingle coastal path, some pavement · Time: 2–2.5 hours walking · Start: Whitstable Harbour (train: Whitstable station, 15 min walk) · End: Herne Bay Pier (train: Herne Bay station, 10 min walk) · Dogs: Yes, welcome year-round on most of the route

Start at Whitstable Harbour — arrive early for oysters or coffee at the harbour cafés before setting off.

Getting there

The walk works best as a one-way trip by train. Take Southeastern services from London Victoria or St Pancras into Whitstable (approx 1h 20min from St Pancras), walk east to Herne Bay, and catch the return train. By car, park in Herne Bay and take the train one stop to Whitstable to start — parking in Herne Bay is easier and cheaper at weekends.

The route

Stage 1 — 1.5 miles

Whitstable Harbour to Tankerton

The path follows shingle east from the harbour. On your right is The Street — a natural shingle ridge extending half a mile out to sea at low tide. Shortly after, the famous Tankerton beach huts begin: painted in heritage colours and facing west over the estuary, best in late afternoon golden light. Tankerton Slopes — the green lawns above the beach — are perfect for picnics and kite-flying.

Left: the shingle shore looking east. Right: Tankerton's beach huts in late afternoon light.

Best food stop on the route

JoJo's Restaurant — Marine Parade, Tankerton CT5 2BE

The best food stop on the walk. JoJo's sits at the top of Tankerton Slopes looking over the estuary and serves a short but excellent menu of seasonal dishes. Lunch is the right meal here. Book ahead at weekends — it fills up fast with walkers who know the route.

Stage 2 — 1.5 miles

Swalecliffe and Hampton Pier

After Tankerton the path continues through Swalecliffe, crossing Swalecliffe Brook and passing Long Rock — a small nature reserve home to sea birds and the rare Fisher's estuarine moth. Hampton Pier is a quiet fishing spot popular with locals. The views east from here, with Herne Bay's Victorian skyline growing on the horizon, are among the best on the walk.

Looking east from Hampton Pier — the Thames Estuary opens out and Herne Bay appears ahead.

Stage 3 — 2 miles

Herne Bay Pier and finish

The approach to Herne Bay is along the seafront promenade. The town's 80-foot Victorian Clock Tower — one of the earliest freestanding purpose-built clock towers in the UK, built in 1837 — announces your arrival. Herne Bay Pier stretches into the estuary: the detached head visible some distance from shore is what remains after a storm destroyed the centre section. Walk to the end for a coffee and the best views back along the coast you've just walked.

Where to eat and drink in Herne Bay

The Four Fathoms on the High Street (CT6 5LH) was voted Kent Life Pub of the Year and is exactly the right place to end a coastal walk: a large courtyard garden, good food, and a comfortable welcome. Beer on the Pier is the obvious choice if you want a drink at the end of the pier itself before catching the train.

Extending the walk

This stretch is part of the Saxon Shore Way, a 163-mile long-distance route from Gravesend to Hastings. Extending east from Herne Bay to Reculver — about 4 miles — is arguably more dramatic, with the twin towers of Reculver Roman fort appearing on the horizon. From Reculver, the Thanet Coastal Path links onward toward Birchington, Margate and Broadstairs.

Good to know

Comfortable walking shoes are fine — boots unnecessary except in wet weather. Almost entirely flat. Dogs welcome on all but a short stretch near a holiday park at Swalecliffe.
