The 7 Best Beaches on the Kent Coast — a Local's Guide

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏖 Beaches📍 Kent coast

Kent has over 350 miles of coastline and more than 50 beaches, with nine holding the Blue Flag award for water quality, safety and facilities in 2025. But the finest stretches aren't always the ones on the tourist boards. We've walked the lot, talked to the locals, and picked the seven that genuinely deliver — dramatic, peaceful, and never far from a great pub.

Practical note — tides matter here

Several of Kent's best beaches are tidal. Botany Bay and Kingsgate Bay can see sand disappear entirely at high tide. Always check before you go — tidetimes.org.uk and the Met Office app are both free and reliable.

1. Botany Bay, near Broadstairs

The chalk stacks at Botany Bay are most dramatic at low tide on a clear morning. Photo: Andras Stefuca / Pexels

If you want one photograph that sums up the Kent coast at its most cinematic, walk down to Botany Bay at low tide on a clear morning. The chalk stacks — those great white columns left behind as the cliff slowly erodes — are among the most striking formations in the south-east, and the sandy bay between them is genuinely beautiful. The rock pools at the base of the cliffs are some of the richest in Thanet, drawing fossil hunters as well as families.

The essentials — Botany Bay

Botany Bay, Cliftonville, Broadstairs

Sandy cove backed by dramatic chalk cliffs — one of Kent's most photographed beaches.

Marine Drive, Cliftonville, Broadstairs CT10 3LG
Getting there: Car park on Botany Road, 10 min walk down cliff path. By train: Broadstairs station (15 min walk).
Best for: Chalk stacks, fossil hunting, rock pools. Dogs: Restricted May–Sep, unrestricted Oct–Apr.

2. Kingsgate Bay, Broadstairs

Kingsgate Bay's chalk sea arch and caves are best explored at low tide.

Tucked between Margate and Broadstairs, Kingsgate Bay is Botany Bay's slightly wilder sibling. The chalk cliffs are just as dramatic, but at low tide the beach reveals a genuine sea arch and a series of caves irresistible to anyone with a torch. The bay stays quieter than Viking Bay because it's harder to find — entirely the point. From here you can walk north to Botany Bay and south toward Joss Bay, the best surf beach in Thanet.

The essentials — Kingsgate Bay

Kingsgate Bay, Broadstairs

Sea caves, a chalk arch, and cliff walks — quieter than Viking Bay year-round.

Kingsgate Avenue, Broadstairs CT10 3QA
Park on Kingsgate Avenue, short footpath to beach. Joss Bay Surf School 5 min walk south. Dogs welcome year-round.

3. Viking Bay, Broadstairs

Viking Bay is Broadstairs at its most quintessentially British — the beach huts and golden sand are a classic combination.

Broadstairs is the classic Kent seaside town — Charles Dickens had a summer home here and you can see why. Viking Bay is the centrepiece: a neat horseshoe of golden sand backed by candy-coloured beach huts, a promenade of independent cafés, and cliffs that glow white in afternoon sun. Blue Flag awarded and lifeguarded in summer.

The essentials — Viking Bay

Viking Bay, Broadstairs

Blue Flag beach, beach huts, lifeguards in summer — Broadstairs' showpiece bay.

Broadstairs town centre CT10 1HX
Pay-and-display parking in town. Direct trains from London St Pancras (1h 20min). For Louisa Bay (quieter alternative) walk south along the cliff.

4. Tankerton Beach, Whitstable

Tankerton's west-facing beach huts catch the full force of a Thames Estuary sunset.

Tankerton is Whitstable's eastern stretch — a long shingle beach lined with the most photogenic beach huts in Kent, facing west over the estuary. On a clear autumn evening this is one of the finest free views on the coast, the light coming gold toward the ghostly Maunsell Sea Forts at Red Sands. At low tide, The Street — a natural shingle ridge — extends half a mile out to sea. Walking it with water on both sides is an entirely memorable experience.

The essentials — Tankerton Beach

Tankerton Beach, Whitstable

Famous beach huts, The Street at low tide, and some of the best sunsets on the coast.

Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE
Parking on Marine Parade. Whitstable station 1.5 miles. Continue east 5 miles to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way.

5. Samphire Hoe, Dover

Samphire Hoe sits at the foot of the White Cliffs of Dover — access is via a tunnel through the chalk.

This one has an extraordinary back story. Samphire Hoe is a nature reserve created from nearly five million cubic metres of chalk excavated to build the Channel Tunnel. Ecologists landscaped it into an undulating coastal shelf and seeded it with wildflowers. Today it hosts over 200 plant species, 220 bird species, and 380 moths. The access road runs through a cliff tunnel, giving arrival a mildly dramatic feeling.

The essentials — Samphire Hoe

Samphire Hoe Nature Reserve, Dover

A remarkable conservation success story beneath the White Cliffs — wheelchair accessible, wildlife-rich, and free.

Western Heights, Dover CT17 9FL
Free car park. Access via cliff tunnel off the A20 — follow signs from Western Docks roundabout. Gate locked at dusk.

6. Stone Bay, Broadstairs

A quiet curve of flat sands just a short walk from Broadstairs town centre. Stone Bay sits sheltered by chalk cliffs creating a slightly warmer microclimate. Blue Flag and Seaside Award both held, it stays noticeably calmer than Viking Bay even at peak summer. Families love it for rock pooling; dog owners for year-round unrestricted access.

The essentials — Stone Bay

Stone Bay, Broadstairs

Stone Road, Broadstairs CT10 1EF
Small car park at the top of the cliff steps. Dogs welcome unrestricted all year. Blue Flag and Seaside Award holder.

7. Sandwich Bay

Access is via a private toll road through a golf course estate — a small fee keeps casual visitor numbers low and the atmosphere wonderfully private. The bay is a long sweep of flat sands and tidal pools backed by dunes. The northern end borders the River Stour and is one of the finest spots on the Kent coast for migrating waders. Pack a thermos — there are no facilities on the beach.

The essentials — Sandwich Bay

Sandwich Bay, Sandwich

Sandwich Bay Road, Sandwich CT13 9PF
Toll road access (small fee per car). Dogs welcome year-round. No facilities on beach — bring everything you need.

The Best Fish & Chip Shops on the Kent Coast — We Ate Them All

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🍟 Food & Drink📍 Kent coast

There is an argument — and we're willing to make it — that a truly great plate of fish and chips on the coast of Kent on a grey October afternoon is one of the finest experiences available in England. Proper batter. Thick chips. Salt air coming through the door. We ate our way from Whitstable in the north to Folkestone in the south.

How we chose these

We visited anonymously, paid our own way, and weighed batter quality, fish freshness, chip thickness, sourcing, and value. We also note the ones to skip.

Whitstable Harbour — start of any serious Kent seafood tour.

Whitstable

⭐ Our top pick in Whitstable — since 1962

VC Jones — Traditional Fish & Chips

A genuine institution. The Times has rated it one of the best fish and chip shops in the UK. VC Jones is one of the last chippies in the south of England still frying in beef dripping, maintaining a tradition that predates the Second World War. A three-generation family business. The fat gives the batter a depth and richness that vegetable oil cannot replicate. Food is donated to local projects and cooking fat is recycled. Queue expected at weekends. Cash preferred.

25 Harbour Street, Whitstable CT5 1AH
Tel: 01227 272703 · vcjones.co.uk
Mon–Sat 11:30am–8pm · Sun 12–7pm (hours seasonal, check website)

Also excellent in Whitstable

Ossie's Best Fish and Chips

Sunday Times mentioned and multiple Tripadvisor Travellers' Choice awards. The batter is thin and genuinely crisp — the kind that shatters rather than bends. Generous portions. A short walk from Tankerton Beach makes this the ideal eat-then-walk evening stop. The 2024 Tripadvisor Travellers' Choice Award winner.

205 Tankerton Road, Whitstable CT5 2AT
Tel: 01227 266600 · ossiesfishandchips.com
Tue–Sun 12–8pm (table bookings recommended)

Upmarket option — Whitstable's oldest restaurant

Wheelers Oyster Bar

Not a chippy in the traditional sense, but the fish is superb — served from behind a distinctive pink-and-blue Victorian shopfront that's been here since 1856. The kitchen now turns out lobster lasagne and scallops alongside battered fish. Just four tables inside. BYOB (no corkage). The Foodie Travel Guide calls it "a near mystical seafood dining experience."

8 High Street, Whitstable CT5 1BQ
Tel: 01227 273311 · wheelersoysterbar.com
Mon–Tue 10:30am–9pm · Thu–Fri 10:15am–9:30pm · Sat 10am–10pm · Wed & Sun: Closed. Bookings essential.

Left: a proper battered cod — the benchmark. Right: Whitstable harbour on a summer evening.

Broadstairs

⭐ Michelin Guide listed — Broadstairs

Kebbells Seafood Bar & Restaurant

Overlooking Viking Bay, Kebbells isn't doing traditional fish and chips — it's doing something better. Small and large sharing plates with a Mediterranean influence, seasonal menus built around locally sourced fish. The stone bass is the dish people come back for. Listed in the Michelin Guide and winner of the Kent Food & Drink Awards Restaurant of the Year. The terrace in summer, looking down over the bay, is one of the best dining spots in Thanet. Book well ahead at weekends.

8 Victoria Parade, Broadstairs CT10 1QS
Tel: 01843 319002 · kebbells.com
Tue–Fri 12–3pm & 6–9pm · Sat–Sun 12–9pm · Mon: Closed. Reservations recommended.

Margate

Margate beach — Peter's Fish Factory is on The Parade, steps from the sand since 1984.

⭐ Our top pick in Margate — since 1984

Peter's Fish Factory

A Margate institution since 1984. On the beach, always busy, and the fish and chips are exactly what they should be: fresh, hot, unpretentious, and good value. The queue is both a warning and a recommendation. Come early or late — mid-afternoon on a summer Saturday is not the moment.

12 The Parade, Margate CT9 1EZ
Open daily 11am–11pm · Takeaway and eat-in available · Outdoor seating facing the beach

Michelin Green Star · For something different — Margate

Angela's (and Dory's)

Angela's is a Michelin Green Star-awarded small seafood restaurant — the award recognising exceptional sustainable practices. No meat on the menu. All seafood sourced from local dayboats, supply chain largely plastic-free. The daily changing menu follows what the boats bring in each morning. Its sister Dory's is a seafood bar two doors along, serving raw, pickled and baked plates, walk-ins only. Both are outstanding.

Angela's: 21 The Parade, Margate CT9 1EX
Tel: 01843 319978 · angelasofmargate.com
Tue–Sat: Lunch from 12pm, Dinner from 6pm. Bookings recommended.
Dory's (wine bar/seafood): Thu–Mon 12–9pm · Walk-ins only

Folkestone

Folkestone Harbour — The Smokehouse is on Back Street, beside the historic Stade fishing quarter.

⭐ Our top pick in Folkestone — one of The Times' top 30

The Smokehouse

Opened by chef Mark Sargeant (a former Gordon Ramsay associate) in a 150-year-old building beside Folkestone harbour. Battered cod or haddock alongside beef dripping chips, salt and pepper squid to start, and a proper wine list. The Times named it one of the 30 best fish and chip shops in Britain. The five-metre glass frontage lets in the cobbled Stade and harbour views. Fish from Kent day boats.

1–3 Back Street, Folkestone CT19 6NN
Tel: 01303 884718 · thesmokehousefolkestone.co.uk
Tue–Sun 12–4pm & 5–9pm · Mon: Closed

A note on prices in 2026

A standard cod and chips runs £12–£16 across these places. For best value, Peter's Fish Factory in Margate consistently delivers the most for your money. Expect to pay more at Kebbells and Angela's — but these are genuinely different experiences, not just fish and chips.

Whitstable to Herne Bay: the Perfect Kent Coastal Walk

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🚶 Walks📍 North Kent

Five miles. Entirely flat. Trains at both ends. A row of the most photographed beach huts in England, two fine pubs, excellent views across the Thames Estuary, and — on a clear day — the rusting outline of the Red Sands Maunsell Sea Forts standing sentinel in the water. The walk from Whitstable to Herne Bay along the Saxon Shore Way is one of the most satisfying and accessible day walks in the south-east.

The quick facts

Distance: 5 miles (8 km) · Terrain: Flat shingle coastal path, some pavement · Time: 2–2.5 hours walking · Start: Whitstable Harbour (train: Whitstable station, 15 min walk) · End: Herne Bay Pier (train: Herne Bay station, 10 min walk) · Dogs: Yes, welcome year-round on most of the route

Start at Whitstable Harbour — arrive early for oysters or coffee at the harbour cafés before setting off east.

Getting there

The walk works best as a one-way trip by train. Southeastern services run from London Victoria or St Pancras into Whitstable (approx 1h 20min from St Pancras). Walk east to Herne Bay and catch the return train. By car, park in Herne Bay (easier and cheaper at weekends) and take the train one stop to Whitstable to start.

The route

Stage 1 — 1.5 miles

Whitstable Harbour to Tankerton

The path follows shingle east from the harbour. On your right is The Street — a natural shingle ridge extending half a mile out to sea at low tide, worth walking if conditions allow. Shortly after, the famous Tankerton beach huts begin: painted in heritage colours and facing west over the estuary, best in late afternoon golden light. Tankerton Slopes — the green lawns above the beach — are perfect for picnics.

Left: Tankerton's beach huts in the late afternoon. Right: the shingle shore looking east toward Herne Bay.

Best food stop on the route

JoJo's Restaurant

The best food stop on the walk. JoJo's sits at the top of Tankerton Slopes looking over the estuary and serves a short but excellent menu of seasonal dishes — relaxed, confident cooking that Whitstable does better than anywhere. Lunch is the right meal here.

2 Marine Parade, Tankerton, Whitstable CT5 2BE
Book ahead at weekends — it fills up fast with walkers who know the route.

Stage 2 — 1.5 miles

Swalecliffe and Hampton Pier

After Tankerton the path continues through Swalecliffe, crossing Swalecliffe Brook and passing Long Rock — a small nature reserve and Site of Special Scientific Interest home to sea birds and the rare Fisher's estuarine moth. Hampton Pier is a quiet fishing spot. The views east from here, with Herne Bay's Victorian skyline growing on the horizon, are among the best on the walk.

Looking east from Hampton Pier — the Thames Estuary opens out and Herne Bay's clock tower appears ahead.

Stage 3 — 2 miles

Herne Bay Pier and finish

The approach to Herne Bay is along the seafront promenade. The town's 80-foot Victorian Clock Tower — built in 1837, one of the earliest freestanding purpose-built clock towers in the UK — announces your arrival. Herne Bay Pier stretches into the estuary: the detached head visible some distance from shore is what remains after a storm destroyed the centre section. Walk to the end for a coffee and the best views back along the coast you've just walked.

Where to eat and drink in Herne Bay

Best pub at the finish

The Four Fathoms

Voted Kent Life Pub of the Year. A large courtyard garden, good food, and a comfortable welcome that's exactly what a tired and windswept walker needs. Also: Beer on the Pier for a drink at the end of the pier itself before catching the train.

The Four Fathoms: High Street, Herne Bay CT6 5LH
Beer on the Pier: Central Parade, Herne Bay CT6 5JJ

Extending the walk

This stretch is part of the Saxon Shore Way, a 163-mile long-distance route from Gravesend to Hastings tracing the coastline as it existed in Roman times. Extending east from Herne Bay to Reculver — about 4 miles — is arguably more dramatic, with the twin towers of the Roman fort appearing on the horizon. From Reculver, the Thanet Coastal Path links onward toward Birchington, Margate and Broadstairs.

Good to know

Comfortable walking shoes are fine — boots unnecessary except in wet weather. Almost entirely flat. Dogs welcome on all but a short stretch near a holiday park at Swalecliffe.

The Best Dog-Friendly Beaches on the Kent Coast

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🐾 Dogs📍 Kent coast

Kent is one of the best counties in England for dogs on the coast. The shingle beaches are easy on paws, the towns are broadly welcoming to four-legged visitors, and outside the summer season you can often walk for miles without meeting another soul. But seasonal restrictions apply on many of the most popular beaches — knowing the rules before you go saves disappointment.

How dog rules work on Kent beaches

Most Kent beaches operate restrictions between 1 May and 30 September (roughly). During this period, dogs are either banned entirely or confined to a small section. Between October and April, restrictions are lifted and most beaches are fully open. Rules vary beach by beach, and some council boundaries differ slightly — always check the local council website for the current year's rules.

Autumn and winter are the finest seasons for dogs on the Kent coast — unrestricted beaches, empty sands, and low golden light.

Year-round dog beaches (no seasonal restrictions)

These are the genuinely unrestricted beaches — dogs welcome every day of the year, no seasonal bans. They are also, not coincidentally, excellent beaches in their own right.

1. Stone Bay, Broadstairs

The best year-round dog beach in Thanet, and probably in Kent. Stone Bay sits in a sheltered chalk cove just south of Broadstairs town centre — easy to reach on foot, never as busy as Viking Bay, and holding both Blue Flag and Seaside Award status. The chalk cliffs above create a windbreak effect that makes it feel warmer than surrounding beaches. The rock pools are rich and endlessly interesting to dogs with any instinct for investigation. Unlike many Thanet beaches, there are no seasonal restrictions at all — Stone Bay is open to dogs every single day of the year.

The essentials — Stone Bay

Stone Bay, Broadstairs

Sheltered chalk cove, Blue Flag, year-round dog access — the best all-seasons dog beach in Thanet.

Stone Road, Broadstairs CT10 1EF
Small car park at the clifftop. Steps down to beach — not suitable for pushchairs. Dogs: unrestricted year-round. No ban, ever.

2. Sandwich Bay

One of Kent's most unusual beaches — access is via a private toll road through a golf estate (small fee per car), which naturally keeps visitor numbers low. The result is a vast, flat sweep of dunes, tidal pools, and sand that feels genuinely wild, backed by the river Stour to the north and the English Channel to the east. Dogs are welcome year-round with no restrictions whatsoever. There are no facilities (no café, no toilets, no bins — pack everything out), but the space and the birdlife more than compensate. For a dog that loves to run, Sandwich Bay is about as good as it gets on this stretch of coast.

The essentials — Sandwich Bay

Sandwich Bay, Sandwich

Wild, private, unlimited space — dogs unrestricted year-round. No facilities, but pack a flask and it's perfect.

Sandwich Bay Road, Sandwich CT13 9PF
Toll road access (small fee). Dogs welcome year-round, no ban, no lead requirements. Bring water, bags, and everything else you need.

3. Kingsdown Beach, near Deal

A long, quiet shingle beach running south from the village of Kingsdown toward Walmer. It's one of the least visited stretches of the Kent coast — no facilities, no car park right on the beach, and no summer crowds. Dogs are welcome year-round with no restrictions. The shingle gives way to sand at low tide, and the views across the Channel to France are among the clearest on the coast. The village pub, the Zetland Arms, sits right above the beach and welcomes dogs inside as well as in the garden.

The essentials — Kingsdown Beach

Kingsdown Beach, near Deal

Quiet, shingle, year-round dog access — and one of the best dog-friendly pubs in Kent directly above it.

Beach Road, Kingsdown, Deal CT14 8AF
Limited roadside parking in Kingsdown village. Dogs year-round, no restrictions. The Zetland Arms (pub directly above beach) is dog-friendly inside and out.

4. Samphire Hoe, Dover

Technically not a traditional beach — Samphire Hoe is a nature reserve built from Channel Tunnel spoil, with a flat coastal path rather than a conventional strand. But it is one of the best places on the Kent coast to walk a dog, year-round and without restriction. The reserve is fully flat, wheelchair and dog-accessible, and fenced from the cliff edge. Dogs can be let off the lead in most areas (except the reserve sections closest to the railway line). The scenery — white cliffs towering above, ferries tracking across the Channel below — is genuinely extraordinary.

The essentials — Samphire Hoe

Samphire Hoe Nature Reserve, Dover

Flat, fenced, extraordinary cliff scenery — one of the finest year-round dog walks on the whole Kent coast.

Western Heights, Dover CT17 9FL
Free car park. Access via cliff tunnel off the A20. Dogs welcome year-round, off-lead in most areas. Gate locked at dusk.

Beaches with seasonal restrictions (but excellent in winter)

These beaches ban or restrict dogs between roughly May and September, but are among the very best outside that window — and in winter they are outstanding.

5. Botany Bay, Broadstairs (Oct–Apr only)

Botany Bay is one of Kent's most spectacular beaches — chalk stacks, rock pools, sand. It's also one of the richest sniffing environments on the coast, with seaweed, chalk, rockpool life, and the occasional stranded cuttlebone. The restriction runs from 1 May to 30 September. Between October and April it's fully open to dogs, and a low-tide winter morning here — chalk stacks catching the frost, beach empty — is one of the finest dog walk experiences in the south-east.

6. Tankerton Beach, Whitstable (Oct–Apr off-lead)

Tankerton's shingle strip is a great dog walk at any time of year — the beach is long enough that you can always find a quiet stretch, the famous beach huts give the walk a distinctly British character, and The Street (the shingle causeway that extends half a mile out to sea at low tide) is an unforgettable experience for a dog who's not alarmed by water on both sides. Dogs are banned from the main stretch in summer but welcome off-lead between October and April. In winter, the west-facing beach catches the low afternoon light magnificently.

Dog-friendly cafés along the coast

Most independent cafés on the Kent coast welcome dogs, especially outside summer. Reliable options: The Neptune pub, Whitstable Harbour (dogs welcome inside and in garden); Whitstable Castle café (garden, dogs welcome); The Funky Mackerel, Deal Pier (outdoor terrace, dogs welcome); Kiosk at Walmer Beach (open year-round, outdoor seating). What to pack for a day's beach walk with a dog

Kent's shingle beaches are generally safe for dogs but there are a few things worth knowing: shingle can get hot in direct sun in summer (check with the back of your hand before letting a dog lie down); some beaches have groynes at low tide that can cut paws; and the tidal range is significant — always check before taking a dog near the base of chalk cliffs, where rock falls are more common than they look. A long lead is useful for beaches with livestock access restrictions nearby, and a collapsible water bowl is worth carrying from May onwards when fresh water is harder to find.

Deal: the Best Town on the Kent Coast You Haven't Visited Yet

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏘️ Towns📍 East Kent

Deal is quietly becoming one of the most talked-about small towns on the south coast. It has everything the more famous Kent seaside towns have — Georgian architecture, a decent pier, pebble beaches, good pubs — but without the day-tripper crowds that make Whitstable difficult on a summer Saturday. It's the kind of place where you can still get a table at lunch without a booking, where the coffee shop knows your name by your second visit, and where the house prices tell you it's been discovered by people who know what they're looking for.

Getting to Deal

Direct trains from London St Pancras take about 1h 40min via Folkestone or Ramsgate. By car, Deal is just off the A258 between Dover and Sandwich — about 15 minutes from the M20. Parking is easy and mostly free on the residential streets west of the seafront.

Deal's fishing boats are still working boats — the beach launch tradition dates back centuries.

What makes Deal different

Most Kent seaside towns pivoted hard to tourism decades ago. Deal didn't. It has a working fishing beach — boats are still launched from the shingle in the old way, and fresh fish is sold directly from the boats on the beach. The town centre is a genuine high street with independent butchers, bakers, a proper hardware shop, and a record store (Pilot). The pubs are locals' pubs that happen to welcome visitors, not visitor pubs with locals' prices.

The seafront is also unusual. There's no promenade funfair, no amusement arcades, no candyfloss stalls. What there is: a long, flat shingle beach good for walking; a Victorian pier that's one of only a handful of intact fishing piers left in England; the ruins of a Napoleonic-era military barracks at Walmer; and the extraordinary time-capsule of Deal Castle, built by Henry VIII in the shape of a Tudor rose and still one of the most intact artillery castles in England.

Where to eat

⭐ Our top pick — Deal's best restaurant

Frog & Scot

A Franco-Scottish bistro that sounds like a joke and delivers something genuinely excellent. A short, seasonal menu built around Kent and Scottish produce — typically five or six starters, five or six mains. The cooking is confident and unfussy: moules marinière done properly, Shetland salmon, a rotating selection of local cheeses. BYO wine (minimal corkage) keeps the bill reasonable. It fills up fast and doesn't take bookings online — call or turn up early.

86 High Street, Deal CT14 6EG
Tel: 01304 379444
Tue–Sat: Lunch 12–2pm, Dinner 6–9pm · Sun lunch only · Mon: Closed. Bookings recommended.

Best seafront café

The Strand

Directly on the seafront, housed in a listed Georgian building with views straight out to the Goodwin Sands. The Strand does the things a good beach café should do — proper coffee, freshly made sandwiches, excellent fish finger baps — but does them better than you'd expect. The sun terrace in summer is the best outdoor seat in Deal. In winter, the interior with its original Georgian windows looking out to sea is almost better.

The Strand, Deal CT14 7AA
Open daily from 9am. Dogs welcome on the terrace. Walk-ins only.

Where to drink

Best pub — Deal

The Ship Inn

The best pub in Deal, possibly the best pub in East Kent. A genuine free house on the seafront — no chain ownership, no fruit machines, no background music loud enough to prevent conversation. The beer range changes constantly and leans hard toward local Kent breweries: Goacher's, Ramsgate Brewery, Old Dairy. The food is a short, honest menu that changes daily. In summer the upstairs terrace looks directly out over the English Channel toward France. In winter, the coal fire is lit and the regulars are exactly the kind of company you want in a pub on a January evening.

The Zetland Arms, Kingsdown

Technically in the village of Kingsdown rather than Deal itself, but only a 25-minute clifftop walk south along the coastal path. The Zetland Arms sits directly above Kingsdown Beach with a terrace that looks straight across the water. It's a proper pub — good local ales, simple food, no pretension — and the walk there along the white-cliff path from Deal Pier is one of the finest 45-minute walks in Kent. Dogs welcome inside and out.

Open daily from 12pm. Dogs welcome. Terrace has direct sea views.

What to see and do

Historic site — Deal

Deal Castle

One of the finest Tudor military buildings in England, and criminally under-visited. Henry VIII built Deal Castle in 1540 as part of a chain of artillery fortresses to defend against a French invasion — the shape, seen from above, is a perfect Tudor rose. The stonework is remarkably intact, the chambers and tunnels are explorable, and the moat is still there. English Heritage manages it and entry is under £10. On a weekday outside summer you can often have the whole place almost to yourself, which makes it feel genuinely eerie in the best possible way.

Victoria Road, Deal CT14 7BA
Open Apr–Oct daily 10am–6pm · Nov–Mar Sat & Sun only 10am–4pm
Adult entry approx £9. English Heritage members free. Dogs allowed in grounds but not inside.

Historic site — Walmer, 1 mile south

Walmer Castle & Gardens

A mile south of Deal along the seafront path, Walmer Castle is Deal's quieter and in some ways more interesting counterpart. Also built by Henry VIII, it was later converted into the official residence of the Lord Warden of the Cinque Ports — a position once held by the Duke of Wellington, whose rooms and effects remain on display. The formal gardens, redesigned in the 19th century, are genuinely beautiful in spring and summer. The combined Deal–Walmer walk along the seafront and back through the town is about 4 miles and takes in the best of both places.

Kingsdown Road, Walmer, Deal CT14 7LJ
Open Apr–Oct daily 10am–6pm. Combined ticket with Deal Castle available. Gardens free to visit separately.

Deal Pier: a proper fishing pier

Deal Pier is one of only a handful of traditional fishing piers left in England that is still used primarily for fishing rather than entertainment. There are no amusements, no arcades, no dodgems — just a 300-metre concrete walkway extending into the Channel with a kiosk at the end selling tea and bait. In summer, the pier is lined with anglers catching bass, sole, and mackerel from the beach end and cod further out. The views back to the town from the pier head — the Georgian seafront, the castle, the shingle beach — are the best views of Deal there are.

The best time to visit Deal

September and October are exceptional — the summer visitors have gone, the weather is still reasonable, and the town relaxes back into itself. The seafood is at its best (bass season is September–November), the pubs are quieter, and the light over the Channel in early autumn is the best photographic light on the coast. If you come in summer, arrive on a weekday and before 11am to park easily.

Deal's back streets are full of well-preserved Georgian terraces — the town was prosperous enough to build well, quiet enough that it was never knocked down and rebuilt.

A day itinerary for Deal

Morning: Arrive by train, walk the seafront south to Walmer Castle (1 mile). Explore the castle and gardens, then walk back along Deal beach. Stop at The Strand for coffee and a fish finger bap looking out to the Goodwin Sands.

Afternoon: Deal Castle (30–45 min). Browse the high street — Pilot Records is worth twenty minutes of any music lover's time. Walk the pier to the end and back.

Evening: The Ship Inn for local ales, then Frog & Scot for dinner (book ahead). Last train to London from Deal around 9.30pm — check timetable in advance.

Margate: Everything You Need to Know Before You Go

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🏘️ Towns📍 Thanet, Kent

Margate has pulled off one of the more unlikely transformations in British seaside history. A town that was genuinely down on its luck fifteen years ago — arcades half-empty, hotels shuttered, the famous Dreamland fairground a ghost of itself — has quietly become one of the most interesting places to spend a weekend on the English coast. The Turner Contemporary gallery was the catalyst, but the story is more complicated and more interesting than that.

Margate in one sentence

A traditional British seaside town where the candyfloss stalls and the Michelin-listed restaurants now exist on the same street, and both are doing good business.

Margate Main Sands — one of the finest town beaches in England, and free to use year-round.

The beach

Margate Main Sands is the starting point for understanding what the town is. A wide arc of golden sand curving between the stone pier and the harbour arm — it's a genuinely excellent beach. Blue Flag quality, gently shelving, safe for children, and backed by the Dreamland big wheel and a row of seafront amusements that feel, in context, more charming than tacky. At low tide the sand extends further than you'd expect. In summer it gets busy; arrive before 11am or after 4pm for breathing room.

The harbour arm itself is worth a separate visit. A Victorian stone pier extending into the bay, it was converted into a food and drink destination and is now lined with independent units — a Neapolitan pizza trailer, a natural wine bar, a proper coffee kiosk — and at weekends fills with exactly the mix of locals, day-trippers and second-homers that defines the new Margate. The views back to the town from the end are some of the best of any harbour arm in the south-east.

Turner Contemporary

The gallery opened in 2011 on the site where J.M.W. Turner used to board as a child, looking out over the bay he credited as the formative influence on his understanding of light. The building — designed by David Chipperfield — is deliberately understated from the outside, a series of interlocking white pavilions that catch the coastal light rather than competing with it. Inside, the galleries are large, naturally lit, and consistently excellent. Turner Contemporary doesn't have a permanent collection; it programmes loan exhibitions from the major national collections and commissions new work. Entry is free. The gift shop is worth a look.

The essentials — Turner Contemporary

Turner Contemporary, Margate

Free entry. One of the most important contemporary art spaces outside London.

The Rendezvous, Margate CT9 1HG
Open Tue–Sun 10am–5pm · Closed Monday · Free entry always
Train: Margate station 5 min walk · Parking: Fort Hill car park nearby

The Old Town

Behind the seafront, Margate's Old Town is where the independent scene is concentrated. King Street, Market Street and the surrounding lanes are now densely packed with record shops, vintage clothing, ceramics studios, independent bookshops, and a rotating cast of good small restaurants. It has the feel of a place that's been discovered but not yet overrun — the rents are still low enough for genuinely independent businesses to survive, which gives it an authenticity that more expensive coastal towns have lost.

The Shell Grotto deserves a paragraph of its own. Discovered in 1835 when someone was digging a duck pond, it is a subterranean passageway whose walls are entirely covered in shells — 4.6 million of them, arranged in intricate patterns of unknown origin and unknown age. Nobody knows who built it or why. It has been carbon dated to at least the early 18th century but could be much older. It's open to visitors, genuinely atmospheric, and one of the strangest things you'll find on the English coast.

Dreamland

Dreamland is Margate's historic amusement park, restored and reopened in 2015 as a heritage fairground and entertainment venue. The rides are deliberately retro — restored vintage attractions alongside newer additions — and the site hosts concerts, club nights and food markets throughout the summer. It divides opinion: some find the heritage angle forced; others love it. Either way, the big wheel gives the best elevated view of the bay and it is, whatever its contradictions, operating again rather than derelict. Worth at least a walk through.

Where to eat

⭐ Best restaurant in Margate

Bottega Caruso

A Sicilian delicatessen and restaurant that opened to immediate and justified acclaim. Antipasti plates built from genuinely excellent Italian imports, pasta made fresh each morning, wines from small Sicilian producers. The room is small and fills up fast. The meatballs in ragù are the dish people come back for. It operates as a deli during the day so you can pick up bread, cheese and cured meat for a beach picnic and return for dinner without feeling like you've been to different places.

Wed–Sun: Lunch 12–3pm, Dinner 6–9pm · Mon & Tue: Closed
Bookings recommended at weekends.

Best for fish — Margate Old Town

Angela's & Dory's

Angela's is the Michelin Green Star-awarded seafood restaurant that put Margate on the serious food map. No meat, all fish sourced from local dayboats, a menu that changes daily based on what came in that morning. Dory's next door is the more casual wine bar sibling — walk-ins only, small plates of raw and cured fish, excellent natural wine list. Both are outstanding. Angela's needs advance booking; Dory's rewards turning up on a quiet Thursday.

Getting there & when to go

Margate is 1h 20min from London St Pancras on the high-speed Southeastern service. This is genuinely fast enough for a day trip, though a weekend gives you time to do it properly. By car it's about 1h 40min from central London via the M2/A2. Parking in Margate is cheap and easy outside summer — the Fort Hill multi-storey is the most convenient for the seafront and Old Town.

The best months are May, June and September. July and August are busy and the Old Town restaurants are harder to get into. October through March sees the visitor numbers drop dramatically but the town doesn't close — Dreamland runs reduced hours, Turner Contemporary stays open, and the Old Town independents are largely year-round. A winter weekend in Margate, with the beach to yourself and the Shell Grotto lit by a January afternoon, is one of the finer off-season coastal experiences in the south-east.

One thing people get wrong about Margate

They come for the gallery, eat at one good restaurant, and miss the Old Town entirely. The Old Town is the point. Give it at least two hours — walk every street, look in every window. That's where the real Margate is.

Whitstable: The Complete Guide to Kent's Coolest Seaside Town

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🏘️ Towns📍 North Kent

Whitstable is the town that quietly became fashionable while nobody was looking. Long before food tourism was a thing, people were driving down the A299 for oysters, sitting on the shingle in the cold, and calling it a good weekend. The town has always had this quality — a genuine, unperformed character that resists the twee seaside-town aesthetic without trying to. The beach huts are iconic, the harbour is working and real, and the high street is overwhelmingly independent.

Whitstable in one sentence

The original Kent seaside destination — oysters, beach huts, proper fish, an independent high street and a harbour that still earns its living.

The Oysters

Whitstable natives are flat, briny, and smaller than their Pacific cousins — they've been harvested from this stretch of the Thames estuary since Roman times. The Whitstable Oyster Company (Royal Native Oyster Stores, Horsebridge Road, CT5 1BU) is the obvious starting point: half a dozen natives with a glass of Muscadet on the terrace is one of the better things you can do on a Kent weekend. Squeeze a wedge of lemon, tip the shell, and don't chew — just let it go.

For something less formal, Wheelers Oyster Bar on the High Street (8 High Street, CT5 1BQ — no card, cash only, tiny) has been here since 1856. It seats about ten people. Book ahead or accept that you'll queue.

The Beach and Harbour

Whitstable's beach is shingle, not sand, which filters out families looking for bucket-and-spade territory and leaves behind people who just want to sit and look at the sea. The beach huts — pastel-painted, stacked along the shore — are the town's most photographed feature and justifiably so. Walk west from the harbour along the Street (a natural shingle causeway that emerges at low tide) for the best views back to the town.

The harbour is small and genuine. Fishing boats still come in with their catch, and the Harbour Market on weekends sells local produce alongside the inevitable artisan bread. The Old Neptune pub sits directly on the shingle — no garden, no terrace, just the beach — and it's one of the better pubs in Kent by virtue of its position alone.

Where to Eat and Drink

The Sportsman
Faversham Road, Seasalter CT5 4BP · 01227 273370

A Michelin-starred pub in a converted roadhouse on the marsh road between Whitstable and Faversham. Stephen Harris's cooking is rooted in the landscape — the butter is churned in-house, the fish comes from the boats you can see from the window. One of the best restaurants in England. Book months ahead.

JoJo's
2 Marine Parade, Tankerton CT5 2BE · 01227 274591

A short walk east along the seafront to Tankerton. Tapas-style small plates, strong on seafood, brilliant in summer when the windows are open and you can watch the tide come in over the Street. Book ahead in season.

The Old Neptune
Marine Terrace, CT5 1EJ

On the beach, literally. Ales, simple food, and one of the best pub positions anywhere on the English coast. Go at sunset in October when the crowds have gone.

Getting There and Around

Whitstable is 60 miles from London. By train from St Pancras via HS1 to Faversham (about 60 minutes), then a connecting service to Whitstable — or direct from Victoria in around 90 minutes. By car, it's the A2/M2 then A299. Parking is difficult in summer; park at the Gorrell Tank (free with a ticket on the walk in) and walk to the harbour. The town is entirely walkable.

When to Go

The Whitstable Oyster Festival in late July brings the town to a halt in the best possible way — oyster-opening competitions, street food, live music. It's worth the crowds. September and October are the sweet spot: the weather is still reasonable, the visitors have gone, and the oysters are at their best (the old rule about months with an R in them has basis in fact — Whitstable natives are better in autumn and winter). Avoid August weekends unless you genuinely enjoy queueing.

Broadstairs: The Best Small Seaside Town on the Kent Coast

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏘️ Towns📍 Thanet, Kent

Broadstairs is the right size. Not so small that everything closes at 5pm, not so large that it's lost its character. It has seven beaches (seven — within walking distance of each other), a Victorian high street that still functions, a Dickens connection it wears lightly, and the kind of fish and chips that make you wonder why you ever ate anywhere else. It is, by some margin, the most complete seaside town in Kent.

Broadstairs in one sentence

Seven beaches, a working Victorian high street, the best fish and chips in Thanet, and the kind of uncomplicated English seaside charm that's increasingly hard to find.

Viking Bay

The main beach is a horseshoe of pale sand enclosed by chalk cliffs, with the town sitting above it on the clifftop. At low tide it's as close to a perfect English beach as exists — the sand is clean, the cliffs are dramatic, the water is genuinely swimmable in summer. The beach huts on the promenade are rented by the season and passed down through families like heirlooms. Bleak House, where Dickens wrote David Copperfield, looks down from the cliff above — you can visit it as a museum.

The Other Beaches

Stone Bay (CT10 1EF), a ten-minute walk north, is better for swimming — calmer water, fewer people, chalk stacks at the northern end that create dramatic rock pools at low tide. Kingsgate Bay (CT10 3QA), another fifteen minutes further, is more dramatic still: a small cove reached by steps cut into the chalk, flanked by crumbling cliff and utterly spectacular. Botany Bay (CT10 3LG) at the far northern end is the showstopper — chalk stacks, clean sand, and the sense that you've found something secret, even though it appears on every Instagram feed in Kent.

Where to Eat

Morelli's Gelato

Kebbells Fish Bar

The fish and chip institution of Broadstairs. Proper battered fish, chips that hold their shape, and a queue that moves quickly. Eat on the seafront wall.

The best pub position in Broadstairs — right on the harbour steps, overlooking Viking Bay. Ales, fresh crab sandwiches, and one of the finer views from any pub beer garden in Kent.

The Dickens Connection

Charles Dickens holidayed in Broadstairs almost every summer between 1837 and 1851, writing much of his best work here. Bleak House (Fort Road, CT10 1HX) is now a museum open in summer. The Dickens House Museum on Victoria Parade (the home of Mary Pearson Strong, the model for Betsey Trotwood) is smaller but more atmospheric. The annual Dickens Festival in June sees the whole town in Victorian costume — genuinely excellent, not at all naff.

Getting There

Broadstairs is 80 miles from London. By train from St Pancras via HS1 to Ramsgate, then one stop — about 80 minutes total. Direct trains from Victoria take around 100 minutes. By car, A2/M2 then A253. Parking is pay-and-display throughout town — arrive before 10am in summer.

Folkestone: The Kent Coast's Most Interesting Town

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 9 min read🏘️ Towns📍 South Kent

Folkestone had a difficult few decades after the Channel Tunnel took its ferry traffic and the town struggled to find a new identity. What emerged — slowly, unevenly, but unmistakably — is one of the most interesting places on the Kent coast. The Creative Quarter has filled the Victorian Old Town with artists' studios, independent restaurants, and a triennial arts festival that brings major international work to public spaces across the town. The Harbour Arm has been transformed into one of the better food and drink destinations in the south-east. And the town still has an edge that the tidier Kent resorts have long since lost.

Folkestone in one sentence

A former ferry port that reinvented itself as a centre for art and independent culture — rough around the edges, genuinely interesting, and far cheaper than anywhere comparable.

The Harbour Arm

The Victorian stone pier that once loaded passengers onto cross-Channel ferries has been converted into a food and drink destination that actually works. Shipping containers house independent restaurants and bars; the old lighthouse at the end has been turned into a champagne bar. On summer evenings it fills up with people eating tacos and watching the sun set over the Channel — it sounds contrived but isn't. The Harbour Arm (CT20 1QH) is open April to October, weekends only out of season.

The Creative Quarter

The Old High Street — formerly derelict, now regenerated — is the centre of Folkestone's creative scene. Studios, galleries, and independent shops occupy the Victorian buildings on either side. The Quarterhouse arts venue (Mill Bay, CT20 1BN) programmes theatre, music, and events year-round. The Triennial brings major public art installations to the town every three years — previous editions have included work by Tracey Emin and Antony Gormley.

Where to Eat and Drink

The Smokehouse
1–3 Back Street, CT19 6NN · 01303 884718

A wood-fired restaurant in the Old Town doing proper smoked and grilled meat and fish. One of the better dinner options in Kent — book ahead at weekends.

Rocksalt
4–5 Fish Market, CT19 6AA · 01303 212070

Mark Sergeant's seafood restaurant overlooking the old fish market and harbour. The terrace in summer is exceptional. The seafood platter for two is the thing to order if budget allows.

The Ship Inn
Barrack Hill, CT19 6NX · 01303 251081

A proper locals' pub in the Old Town — decent ales, real food, and none of the gastropub posturing. The beer garden has views down to the harbour.

The Leas and the Warren

The Leas is the Victorian clifftop promenade above the town — a wide, grassed esplanade with views across the Channel to France on clear days. The funicular lift (1885, still running) connects it to the Lower Sandgate Road below. East of the town, the Warren is a Site of Special Scientific Interest: a strange, collapsed landscape of landslips and chalk grassland between the cliffs and the sea, excellent for walking and wildlife. Access from the Martello Tower car park at the east end of Folkestone.

Getting There

Folkestone is 70 miles from London. By HS1 from St Pancras to Folkestone Central — 54 minutes, which makes it one of the fastest Kent coast journeys from London. By car, M20 junction 13. The Harbour Arm is a 15-minute walk downhill from the station.

Herne Bay: The Honest Kent Seaside Town That Doesn't Pretend

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🏘️ Towns📍 North Kent

Herne Bay doesn't try to be fashionable and it's better for it. While Whitstable 4 miles to the west has become expensive and self-conscious, Herne Bay remains what it always was: a proper English seaside town with a long promenade, a working pier, an ice cream tradition, and accommodation that won't require a second mortgage. It's ideal for families, genuinely good for walking, and completely underrated.

Herne Bay in one sentence

The unpretentious, affordable alternative to Whitstable — better for families, better for walking, and with one of the finest sunsets on the North Kent coast.

The Pier and Promenade

Herne Bay's pier is one of the longest in England at over a kilometre — though only the shore end and pier head survive intact, the middle section having been demolished. The pier head is an odd, windswept structure with a café that serves reasonable coffee and unreasonable views across the Thames Estuary. The clock tower at the landward end of the pier (1837) is one of the earliest freestanding clock towers in England and worth ten minutes of your time.

The promenade runs for nearly two miles east to west — flat, wide, and excellent for cycling (bikes can be hired from the seafront). At the eastern end, the promenade connects to the path towards Reculver and its ruined twin-towered church, a 4-mile walk with exceptional estuary views throughout.

Reculver Country Park

Reculver (CT6 6SX) is a 4-mile walk or short drive east of Herne Bay. The twin towers of the ruined 12th-century church sit on a clifftop above a Roman fort site, visible for miles in every direction. The beach below is wide, pebbly, and almost always empty. The Smugglers' Inn at Reculver is a reasonable pub; the country park car park is pay-and-display. Allow 2–3 hours for the return coastal walk from Herne Bay.

Where to Eat

Four Fathoms
High Street, CT6 5LH · 01227 363306

The best fish and chip shop in Herne Bay — proper battered fish, good chips, and a café section if you want to sit down. On the main high street, 5 minutes from the beach.

Beer on the Pier
Central Parade, CT6 5JJ

Dungeness: Britain's Only Desert, and Worth Every Kilometre

📅 Updated March 2026🌿 Nature◷ 8 min read📍 Romney Marsh, Kent

Dungeness is not like anywhere else in Britain. Europe's largest expanse of shingle sits at the tip of a low headland pointing into the Channel — a flat, strange, almost lunar landscape of fishing huts, driftwood sculptures, rusting hulks, and wildflowers that shouldn't exist but do. Two nuclear power stations brood over the scene. A narrow-gauge railway runs along the edge. Derek Jarman spent his last years here, creating a garden of stone and sea-kale that has become one of the most visited artist's gardens in England. It is magnificent, unsettling, and entirely unlike the rest of Kent.

Dungeness in one sentence

Europe's largest shingle expanse: a flat, post-apocalyptic landscape of fishing huts, wildflowers, lighthouses, and one of the most extraordinary artists' gardens in England.

Prospect Cottage and Jarman's Garden

Derek Jarman, the filmmaker and artist, moved to Prospect Cottage (Dungeness Road, TN29 9NE) in 1986 after his HIV diagnosis. He created a garden on the surrounding shingle — no soil, no water, just stones, found objects, and salt-tolerant plants. The yellow-painted cottage with its John Donne poem on the exterior wall is one of the most affecting sights on the English coast. The garden is viewable from the public road and at its best in May and June when the valerian, sea kale, and California poppies are in flower. The Art Fund purchased the cottage in 2020 to preserve it. Do not ring the doorbell.

The RSPB Reserve

Dungeness RSPB Nature Reserve (Boulderwall Farm, Dungeness Road, TN29 9PN · 01797 320588) covers over 1,000 hectares of shingle, lakes, and grassland. It's one of the best birdwatching sites in Britain — over 320 species recorded, including regular bitterns, Cetti's warblers, and large flocks of wintering wildfowl. The visitor centre has hides, binoculars for hire, and knowledgeable staff. Entry is free for RSPB members, modest charge otherwise.

The Lighthouses

There are two lighthouses at Dungeness. The old lighthouse (CT-era, 1904) can be climbed for panoramic views — 169 steps, worth every one on a clear day. The new lighthouse (1961, still operational) is the taller structure visible from miles away. Both are distinct and photogenic. The old lighthouse is open to visitors most of the year (check ahead).

The Romney, Hythe and Dymchurch Railway

The world's smallest public railway (one-third full size) has run between Hythe and Dungeness since 1927. Riding it from Hythe to Dungeness and back is one of the stranger and more enjoyable ways to spend a Kent afternoon — the train threads through Romney Marsh, stops at tiny stations, and deposits you at Dungeness station next to the pub. The return journey is 14 miles each way; allow 3–4 hours for the round trip including time at Dungeness. The Britannia pub at Dungeness station (TN29 9NB) is the obvious lunch stop.

Getting There

Dungeness is not on a train line. It is 80 miles from London by car via M20 to Lydd — about 90 minutes. The nearest train stations are Rye (7 miles) and Ashford (15 miles). A car is effectively essential. The car park at the old lighthouse is free. Go on a weekday if possible — weekends bring significant crowds in summer.

Seven Sisters and Beachy Head: The Greatest Coastal Walk in the South

The Seven Sisters — eight chalk cliffs, despite the name — is the most dramatic coastal landscape in the south of England. The walk in one sentence

Four miles of chalk cliff edge above the Channel, eight undulating peaks, the most spectacular coastal scenery in the south of England — and a good pub at either end.

The Route

The classic walk runs from Seaford to Eastbourne (or reverse) via the cliff tops — approximately 10 miles. The western start is Seaford Head car park (BN25 1TP); the eastern end is Eastbourne seafront. The South Downs Way runs the full length. For a shorter version, start at the Seven Sisters Country Park visitor centre at Exceat (BN25 4AD) and walk to Birling Gap (BN20 0AB) — 3.5 miles across the heart of the cliffs, with the Birling Gap Hotel at the end for food and drink.

The Cuckmere Valley section at the western end offers the famous view of the meanders with the cliffs behind — one of the most photographed landscapes in England. The path descends to the valley floor and crosses the river before climbing back to the cliff tops.

The National Trust café and pub at the midpoint of the walk. The terrace has views straight down to the beach below. Accessible by car if you want to split the walk.

A pub in the village 10 minutes inland from Birling Gap. Excellent ales, good food, and a proper village green to sit on. The better option if you want a proper lunch stop.

Beachy Head

The clifftop is accessible by car from the B2103 from Eastbourne, and the Beachy Head pub (BN20 7YA) provides refuelling. Eastbourne is the most elegantly Edwardian resort on the south coast — broad promenades, Victorian hotels, bandstand concerts, and a pier that still earns its living. It's often dismissed as a retirement town and there's truth in that, but Eastbourne does its particular thing — formal English seaside — with more conviction than anywhere else. The seafront between the pier and the Wish Tower (BN21 3NP) is genuinely beautiful. The Towner Art Gallery (Devonshire Park, BN21 4JJ) runs an excellent contemporary programme.

The island has two settlements: West Mersea, which has the oyster sheds, the beach, the sailing club, and most of the population; and East Mersea, which is largely farmland and feels like somewhere much further from London than it actually is.

Mersea in one sentence

A tidal island connected to Essex by a causeway that floods twice daily — home to some of the finest native oysters in England, a serious sailing scene, and an end-of-the-world quietness that's completely at odds with its proximity to London.

The Oysters

Mersea Island has been producing oysters since Roman times — the beds in the Pyefleet Channel between Mersea and the mainland are among the finest in England. The West Mersea Oyster Bar (Coast Road, CO5 8LT · 01206 381600) is the place to go: a proper oyster shed on the beach, with native and rock oysters, local crab, whelks, and whatever else came in that morning. Eat at the outdoor tables with the saltmarsh behind you and the Blackwater in front. The Company Shed (129 Coast Road, CO5 8PA · 01206 382700) is another institution — a small, BYO room with extraordinary fresh seafood. Both require booking in season.

The Strood and the Causeway

The Strood causeway floods for roughly two hours around each high tide. The tidal tables are posted at each end; the local rule is that if you can't see the road, don't attempt it. Being cut off is not dangerous — it just means sitting in the pub for a couple of hours. Check the tide times before you go (tidetimes.org.uk · postcode CO5).

Walking on East Mersea

The eastern end of the island is largely deserted farmland and saltmarsh with footpaths along the sea wall — classic Essex coastal walking. The views across the Colne Estuary to Brightlingsea are exceptional on a clear day. Cudmore Grove Country Park (Bromans Lane, CO5 8UE) at the island's eastern tip has a beach, a café, and access to the sea wall path. The circular walk from West Mersea village to Cudmore Grove and back (7 miles, 3 hours) is one of the best flat coastal walks in Essex.

Where to Stay and Eat

The West Mersea Oyster Bar
Coast Road, CO5 8LT · 01206 381600

Native and rock oysters, fresh crab, lobster in season, and local whelks. One of the finest seafood experiences in Essex. Book ahead or arrive early — the good stuff goes quickly.

The Victory
Coast Road, CO5 8LT · 01206 382907

A pub directly on the waterfront in West Mersea. Decent ales, reasonable food, and the kind of waterside position that makes anything taste better. Gets busy in summer.

Getting There

Mersea Island is 60 miles from London. By car, A12 to Colchester then B1025 south to the island — about 75 minutes from the M25. No direct public transport; the closest bus stop is at Mersea, connecting to Colchester. The causeway is signposted from the B1025. Check tide times before travelling — being stuck on the island is pleasant, but being stuck on the wrong side with a reservation isn't.

Leigh-on-Sea: Cockles, Crabbing, and the Best Thing About the Thames Estuary

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🏘️ Towns📍 Essex

Leigh-on-Sea sits between Southend and Canvey Island on the north shore of the Thames Estuary — but Old Leigh, the original fishing village below the railway line, is a different world from either. A narrow lane runs along the waterfront past cockle sheds, weatherboard pubs, and a still-functioning fishing fleet. The cockle boats go out into the estuary and return with their catch; the sheds sell it by the pint, still warm. Old Leigh is one of the best pockets of genuine working waterfront left on the north side of the Thames.

Leigh-on-Sea in one sentence

A working fishing village that has somehow survived intact below a prosperous London commuter town — cockle sheds, weatherboard pubs, Thames Estuary mud, and the best shellfish in Essex.

Old Leigh

Old Leigh occupies a narrow strip between the railway embankment and the water. The main lane — The Broadway — runs for about 400 metres past the historic buildings. Osborne Brothers (The Broadway, SS9 2DH) is the most famous of the cockle sheds: fresh cockles, whelks, crab, and jellied eels sold from the hatch. Eat standing at the sea wall or on the beach beyond the sheds. The Pink Shed and Stonebridge Shellfish are good alternatives.

The beach at Old Leigh is shingle and mud — not a swimming beach, but excellent for sitting and watching the estuary traffic. Canvey Island sits opposite; the Kent coast is visible in the distance on a clear day. At low tide the mud extends far out and the smell of the estuary is elemental and memorable.

The Pubs

The Smack
High Street, Old Leigh, SS9 2EP · 01702 478051

The oldest pub in Leigh, named after the fishing smack boats that worked the estuary for centuries. Right on the waterfront, ales well-kept, atmosphere genuine. Go at high tide when the water comes to the door.

The Crooked Billet
High Street, Old Leigh, SS9 2EP · 01702 479677

Slightly east of the Smack, equally good position, equally honest pub. The two pubs and the cockle sheds between them are the entire point of Old Leigh — everything else is context.

New Leigh

The town above the railway line (known locally as New Leigh or just Leigh) has grown into one of the more prosperous commuter towns on the Essex coast — property prices are high, the high street is independent and good quality, and the restaurant scene has improved significantly over the past decade. Broadway Market has several good options; the Crosse Keys and Elms are well-regarded restaurants. The contrast between old and new Leigh — the fishing village below the tracks, the prosperous suburb above — is one of the more interesting social landscapes on the Thames.

Getting There

Leigh-on-Sea is 37 miles from London. By train from Fenchurch Street to Leigh-on-Sea — about 50 minutes. Leigh station is 5 minutes' walk from Old Leigh. By car, A127 then A13 — allow 90 minutes from London. Parking is available in the seafront car parks (pay-and-display) and on the street above the railway line.

Walton-on-the-Naze: The Quiet Essex Seaside Town With the Crumbling Cliffs

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 7 min read🏘️ Towns📍 Essex

Walton-on-the-Naze is the kind of English seaside town that has quietly got on with things without troubling the travel supplements. It has a three-kilometre sandy beach, a pier (the third longest in England at 800 metres), a nature reserve on the Naze headland to the north, and a regular supply of fossil hunters who come for the Red Crag cliffs — one of the richest fossil sites in Britain. It is excellent for families, genuinely good for walking, and completely unpretentious.

Walton in one sentence

A traditional Essex seaside town with a long sandy beach, a proper pier, crumbling fossil cliffs, and the kind of unselfconscious English seaside charm that larger resorts have long since lost.

The Naze

The Naze is the headland immediately north of the town — a Site of Special Scientific Interest of Red Crag cliffs, saltmarsh, and open grassland managed by the Essex Wildlife Trust. The cliffs are eroding rapidly (losing up to 2 metres per year) which continually exposes fossils from the Red Crag deposits: shark teeth, shells, whale bones, and occasional mammal remains. The Naze Tower (Naze Tower Visitor Centre, CO14 8LE), an 18th-century navigational tower, has excellent views from the top. The coastal walk from Walton seafront to the tip of the Naze and back (3 miles) is one of the most interesting short walks in Essex.

The Pier and Beach

Walton Pier (The Parade, CO14 8AB) is 800 metres long — the third longest in England — with amusements at the shore end and a quieter bar and café at the pier head. The beach to the south is sandy and wide, with water quality that is generally excellent. The town is well set up for families — beach huts for hire, a paddling pool, and the kind of seafront that hasn't changed much since the 1970s in the best possible way.

Where to Eat

The Naze Tower Tea Room
Naze Tower, CO14 8LE · 01255 677772

A café in the Naze Tower visitor centre — good coffee, cake, and the best views in Walton. The obvious stop before or after the Naze walk.

Fletchers Fish Bar
31 High Street, CO14 8AH · 01255 675040

The most reliable fish and chips in Walton — proper battered fish, good chips, and a café section. Go for lunch on a weekday to avoid queuing.

Getting There

Walton-on-the-Naze is 75 miles from London. By train from Liverpool Street to Walton-on-the-Naze via Colchester and Thorpe-le-Soken — about 90 minutes, with a change at Thorpe. By car, A12 to Colchester then B1033 — about 90 minutes from the M25. The seafront car park (The Parade, CO14 8AB) is pay-and-display. The town, pier, and Naze are all within easy walking distance of the station.

Dedham Vale: Constable Country, the Stour Valley, and a Perfect English Landscape

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🌿 Countryside📍 Essex/Suffolk border

Dedham Vale sits on the Essex–Suffolk border in the Stour Valley — an Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty that looks, in certain lights, almost exactly as it did when John Constable painted it in the 1810s and 20s. Flatford Mill, Willy Lott's Cottage, the mill pond, and the meadows beside the Stour are among the most recognisable landscapes in English art. The vale is also excellent walking territory, with gentle river paths and well-maintained routes through water meadows that are exceptional in spring and early summer. It is not a coastal destination in the strict sense, but sits within 10 miles of the Essex coast and pairs perfectly with a day at Mersea or Wivenhoe.

Dedham Vale in one sentence

The landscape that Constable painted and that has barely changed since — the most beautiful river valley on the Essex–Suffolk border, with Flatford Mill, excellent walking, and some of the best village pubs in the east of England.

Flatford Mill and Willy Lott's Cottage

Flatford Mill (East Bergholt, CO7 6UL) is owned by the National Trust and operated as a field studies centre — the mill itself is not publicly accessible, but the mill pond, the bridge, and the view of Willy Lott's Cottage from the east bank are all accessible on foot from the National Trust car park at Flatford (pay-and-display). The scenes are immediately recognisable from Constable's paintings. The National Trust tearoom is in the Granary building; Bridge Cottage (free entry) has information about Constable and the landscape.

The walk along the south bank of the Stour from Flatford to Dedham (2.5 miles, 1 hour) is the finest riverside path in the area — flat, well-maintained, and lined with water meadows that are carpeted with wildflowers in May and June. Return on the north bank for a 5-mile circuit.

The Village of Dedham

Dedham village (CO7 6DE) sits a mile north of Flatford across the meadows. The High Street has a cluster of good independent shops, a gallery, and several pubs. The Sun Inn (High Street, CO7 6DF · 01206 323351) is the best option for food — excellent modern cooking in a handsome building, with a garden that faces the church tower. The arts centre in the old Flemish Weavers' cottage runs a regular programme of exhibitions.

Wivenhoe and the River

Wivenhoe (CO7) — 4 miles south on the Colne estuary — is the perfect complement to Dedham Vale. A small waterfront town with a quay, a working boatyard, good pubs, and the University of Essex campus just up the hill. The Wivenhoe Trail connects the town to Colchester along the river bank (4 miles, easy). The Black Buoy (Black Buoy Hill, CO7 9BS · 01206 822425) is an excellent pub on the quayside.

Getting There

Dedham Vale is 65 miles from London. By train from Liverpool Street to Manningtree (55 minutes via fast train) — Flatford is a 2-mile walk from Manningtree station. Dedham village is 4 miles from Manningtree; a taxi is the practical option. By car, A12 to junction 30 then B1029 — about 70 minutes from the M25. The National Trust car park at Flatford is well-signposted from the B1029. Note: the road to Flatford is very narrow — in summer, park at the top car park and walk down.

Colchester: Britain's Oldest Recorded Town and the Gateway to the Essex Coast

📅 Updated March 2026◷ 8 min read🏘️ Towns📍 Essex

Colchester is the oldest recorded town in Britain — Camulodunum, the first Roman capital of Britannia, founded around AD 49. It has a Norman castle built on Roman foundations, Roman walls that still stand to head height in places, and a high street that has been continuously inhabited for two thousand years. It is also the gateway to the Essex coast — Mersea Island is 8 miles south, Wivenhoe is 4 miles east, and the Tendring coast (Clacton, Walton, Frinton) is 20 miles east. Worth at least a day of anyone's time.

Colchester in one sentence

Britain's oldest recorded town — Roman walls, a Norman castle, an excellent museum, and the best base for exploring the Essex coast and Dedham Vale.

Colchester Castle

Colchester Castle (Castle Park, CO1 1TJ · 01206 282939) is the largest Norman castle keep in Europe — built by William the Conqueror on the foundations of a Roman temple to Claudius. The castle museum inside is excellent and takes 90 minutes to do properly. The Roman foundations in the castle vaults are particularly impressive. The castle park surrounding it is one of the best public parks in Essex — good café, good playground, good views of the Roman walls from the ramparts.

The Roman Walls and Dutch Quarter

Colchester's Roman walls — Balkerne Gate, the largest surviving Roman gateway in Britain, is particularly impressive — survive almost complete and can be walked in around 45 minutes. The Dutch Quarter, north of the High Street, was settled by Flemish weavers in the 16th century and still has a distinct character of narrow lanes and independent shops. Eld Lane and Head Street are the best streets.

Where to Eat and Drink

The Grain Bar
10a Culver Street West, CO1 1JE · 01206 505040

Colchester's best craft beer bar — an excellent rotating selection of Essex and East Anglian breweries, knowledgeable staff, and a relaxed atmosphere. The place to drink in Colchester.

Greyfriars
East Street, CO1 2TX · 01206 500000

A restaurant in a converted medieval friary — good modern cooking using local Essex produce. The Sunday lunch is popular and worth booking ahead.

Lemon Tree
48 St John's Street, CO2 7AD · 01206 767337

A reliable modern British restaurant that has been serving Colchester well for many years. The fish dishes are particularly good — sourced from the coast 15 miles away.

Getting There

Colchester is 60 miles from London. By train from Liverpool Street to Colchester Town — 55 minutes on a fast service. The castle, Dutch Quarter, and high street are all within 10 minutes' walk of the station. By car, A12 — about 65 minutes from the M25. Colchester is an excellent base: Mersea Island (8 miles south), Wivenhoe (4 miles east), Dedham Vale (7 miles north), and the Tendring coast (20 miles east) are all straightforward day trips.
